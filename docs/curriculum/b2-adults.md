# B2 Adults Curriculum

All 12 units of the B2 Adults track, each broken down lesson by lesson.
Every lesson is 25 minutes. Continues directly from B1 Adults — same
12 unit topics, deepened into negotiation-driven, genre-aware
communication, per the B2 pedagogical pile's "instrumental relevance"
driver for this age group.

Lesson shape (negotiation/mediation arc, genuinely different from B1
Adults' scenario-chain shape): **Scenario Briefing (2 min) → Read, main
source sometimes paired with a short second source (6 min) → Discourse
& Language Focus, register and cohesion (5 min) → Negotiate / Mediate,
propose/negotiate/summarize positions for a third party (7 min) →
Write / Synthesize, genre-aware output (3 min) → Upgrade Feedback &
Carry-Forward (2 min)**.

Grammar range: past simple/continuous/perfect for layered narrative and
incident background, present perfect and present perfect continuous
for professional/personal duration and status, full modal nuance
(could, might, would, should, must, may, have to, ought to), first and
second conditional with high control plus selective third conditional
for professional reflection, relative clauses and subordination for
cause/concession/purpose/contrast, and the fuller discourse marker
range (however, in contrast, on the other hand, as a result, overall)
explicitly discussed as register/organization tools.

Genre variation is deliberate across the lesson-type pattern: Lesson 1
status updates (email/message), Lesson 2 incident accounts/reports
(often explicitly not an email), Lesson 3 negotiation emails/messages,
Lesson 4 recommendation notes, Lesson 5 mediation briefs — not a
generic email every time.

Output targets: 150–180 word writing per lesson (up from B1 Adults'
100–140), with a genuine mediation/negotiation task built into every
lesson (not just Lesson 5), always resolving toward a third-party
summary or recommendation.

# Unit 1: Daily Communication and Personal Updates

## Lesson 1: Status Updates in Daily Life

| Part | Plan |
|------|------|
| Time | 25 minutes |
| Main aim | The student can give a clear, B2-level daily-life status update using present perfect and present perfect continuous, adjusting tone for a semi-formal chat or check-in. |
| Materials | short WhatsApp-style chat screenshot, 2-paragraph internal message update, phrase bank for present perfect/continuous status |

| Stage | Time | Online material / activity | Teacher role |
|-------|------|----------------------------|--------------|
| Scenario Briefing | 2 min | Student is told: “You’re updating a colleague/family member about how things have been recently (work, life admin, general situation).” Agree which real-life person/context to imagine. | Clarify scenario, set purpose (update + small request), and agree level of formality. |
| Read (main source, sometimes paired with a short second source) | 6 min | Student reads a short internal update message (e.g., to a team group) where the sender uses present perfect/continuous to report recent actions (“I’ve sorted…”, “We’ve been waiting for…”) and a WhatsApp chat snippet with a more informal status update to a friend. | Guide reading for what information is included/excluded in each, and how tone shifts between the two sources. |
| Discourse & Language Focus (register and cohesion) | 5 min | Student underlines present perfect and present perfect continuous forms and highlights discourse markers like “so far”, “recently”, “in the last few days”, “overall”. They categorise examples by function: progress, ongoing issue, general trend. | Clarify meaning/form and show how aspect + markers structure a concise status update; compare more formal vs informal markers and contractions. |
| Negotiate / Mediate (propose, negotiate, or summarize positions for a third party) | 7 min | Student role-plays a short voice-note style summary to a third person: “Can you tell X what’s been going on?” They must synthesize from both sources (team message + chat) into a coherent 70–90 second spoken status that distinguishes what is done vs still in progress. | Prompt for clarification questions (“So what exactly has already been done?”); push for coherent structure and register suitable for that third person (e.g., manager vs cousin). |
| Write / Synthesize (genre-aware output) | 3 min | Student writes a 150–180 word semi-formal status email to a contact (“Just a quick update on how things have been”), using at least two present perfect and two present perfect continuous forms plus at least three discourse markers. | Support layout (greeting, brief context, 2–3 status points, small closing remark), and nudge register away from chat-style abbreviations. |
| Upgrade Feedback & Carry-Forward | 2 min | Teacher reads out a short extract “as said” and then an upgraded version with tighter aspect and clearer markers (e.g., “I worked a lot recently” → “I’ve been working longer hours over the last few weeks, so I haven’t had much free time”). Student repeats and notes one “update phrase” to carry forward. | Focus upgrade on aspect choice, register (semi-formal), and organizational markers, not just error correction. |

## Lesson 2: What Happened Recently – Incident Background

| Part | Plan |
|------|------|
| Time | 25 minutes |
| Main aim | The student can give a layered narrative of a recent incident in daily life using past simple, past continuous, and past perfect, making the sequence clear for someone who wasn’t there. |
| Materials | short incident description prompt (delivery problem, neighbour issue, etc.), 2-part incident report (informal message + more formal note), timeline template |

| Stage | Time | Online material / activity | Teacher role |
|-------|------|----------------------------|--------------|
| Scenario Briefing | 2 min | Scenario: “A small but important thing went wrong recently (delivery, appointment, neighbour misunderstanding). You need to explain what happened to someone who wasn’t there so they can help.” | Help student choose or imagine a safe, non-sensitive incident; clarify listener (friend, receptionist, building manager). |
| Read (main source, sometimes paired with a short second source) | 6 min | Student reads a short informal message describing the incident and a brief formal incident note (e.g., building log) about the same event. Both use past simple, past continuous, and some past perfect to clarify background vs main events. | Guide student to map the two texts on a timeline: what had already happened, what was going on, what then happened. |
| Discourse & Language Focus (register and cohesion) | 5 min | Student underlines past perfect, past continuous, and past simple forms and highlights markers like “earlier that day”, “while”, “by the time”, “in the end”. They compare how the informal vs formal version handle detail and emotion. | Clarify how past perfect sets background, past continuous sets scene, and past simple marks key steps; discuss register (fewer feelings, more facts in formal note). |
| Negotiate / Mediate (propose, negotiate, or summarize positions for a third party) | 7 min | Student mediates: they must explain the incident to a third party (e.g., concierge, customer service) whose only information is the short formal note. They orally add key details from the informal version in a structured 90–120 second explanation. | Prompt for clarifying and checking questions (“So at that point, where were you?”); encourage the student to rephrase and highlight key facts, not just retell chronologically. |
| Write / Synthesize (genre-aware output) | 3 min | Student writes a concise 150–180 word incident report paragraph (not an email) to attach to a ticket, using all three narrative pasts and 3–4 time markers. | Support genre conventions: neutral tone, no emojis, chronological clarity, paragraphing rather than bullet chat. |
| Upgrade Feedback & Carry-Forward | 2 min | Teacher selects 3–4 original sentences and offers a more B2-like paragraph with better time framing and markers (“At first… Later that evening… By the time the driver arrived…”). Student compares, repeats, and notes one marker they will recycle. | Emphasize layering background vs main event, and tightening narrative flow. |

## Lesson 3: Daily Requests and Negotiation of Favors

| Part | Plan |
|------|------|
| Time | 25 minutes |
| Main aim | The student can negotiate everyday favors and small requests using nuanced modals and polite register (could, would, might, would you mind, I was wondering if…). |
| Materials | mini-scenarios (ask neighbour, colleague, landlord), sample email requesting a small favour, short WhatsApp thread with less-polite version, modal/softener phrase bank |

| Stage | Time | Online material / activity | Teacher role |
|-------|------|----------------------------|--------------|
| Scenario Briefing | 2 min | Scenario: “You need to ask for something inconvenient but reasonable (borrow something, change a time, get help with a task), and you want to sound polite and flexible, not demanding.” | Help student choose a realistic context (work, housing, neighbour) and clarify power relations (peer vs more senior person). |
| Read (main source, sometimes paired with a short second source) | 6 min | Student reads a short polite request email and a more direct chat exchange on the same issue. The email uses modal nuance and hedging; the chat version uses blunt imperatives or “Can you…?” without context. | Guide noticing of tone: which sounds too direct, which sounds overly formal, which is acceptable in their context. |
| Discourse & Language Focus (register and cohesion) | 5 min | Student highlights modals and softeners (“could you possibly…”, “would it be possible to…”, “I was wondering if…”, “might be able to…”, “shouldn’t be a problem, but…”), plus discourse markers for structure (“First…, Also…, In addition…, Overall…”). They classify phrases as opener, reason, request, flexibility, and closing. | Explain register differences (more/less formal), and when to use each; explicitly link markers to organizing polite requests. |
| Negotiate / Mediate (propose, negotiate, or summarize positions for a third party) | 7 min | Role-play: student plays themselves negotiating a small favour with a person who hesitates. They must propose alternatives and show flexibility (“If it’s easier for you, we could…”, “Would another time work?”), then summarize the agreed solution to a third party (“So, we decided that…”). | Play the counterpart, introduce gentle resistance (“I’m quite busy then”), push student to adjust modals/stance and then to summarize clearly for a third person. |
| Write / Synthesize (genre-aware output) | 3 min | Student writes a 150–180 word polite request email or message to a semi-formal contact, including: soft opening, reason, main request with modal nuance, at least one alternative, and a polite closing. | Support genre layout (subject line idea, greeting, sign-off) and ensure tone matches the chosen power distance. |
| Upgrade Feedback & Carry-Forward | 2 min | Teacher shows a “before” sentence from the student (“Can you change the time?”) and a B2 “after” version (“I was wondering if we could move the meeting to… if that works for you”). Student repeats and notes one softener for future use. | Focus upgrade on modal nuance, politeness, and sequencing rather than new grammar. |

## Lesson 4: Everyday Consequences – If This Changes, Then What?

| Part | Plan |
|------|------|
| Time | 25 minutes |
| Main aim | The student can discuss practical consequences in daily life using first, second, and selective third conditional forms to explore options and mild regrets. |
| Materials | short scenario (schedule change, unexpected bill, minor home issue), 2 short texts (a short note with future plans + a personal reflection paragraph), conditional/marker cards |

| Stage | Time | Online material / activity | Teacher role |
|-------|------|----------------------------|--------------|
| Scenario Briefing | 2 min | Scenario: “Something in your daily routine has changed (new schedule, extra shift, transport change). You need to discuss what this means for the rest of your week and maybe what you’d do differently next time.” | Help choose a realistic, non-threatening change; agree which outcomes really matter for the student. |
| Read (main source, sometimes paired with a short second source) | 6 min | Student reads a short planning note from a person explaining a change (“If the bus is late, I’ll…; If I had known earlier, I would have…”) and a brief reflective paragraph where they use second/third conditional for hypothetical alternatives. | Guide student to identify real future actions vs hypothetical/impossible alternatives and mild regrets. |
| Discourse & Language Focus (register and cohesion) | 5 min | Student highlights examples of first, second, and third conditionals, then matches each to “real plan”, “imaginary solution”, or “regret/lesson”. They also underline discourse markers for consequence and contrast (“as a result”, “otherwise”, “on the other hand”, “given that…”). | Clarify functional difference between each conditional type and show how markers signal logical consequences vs contrast. |
| Negotiate / Mediate (propose, negotiate, or summarize positions for a third party) | 7 min | Student and teacher negotiate a small schedule adjustment or solution to the scenario using conditional language (“If we move this to…, we’ll still be able to…”, “If I didn’t have to…, I would…”). Then student summarizes the agreed plan and one alternative for a third person (e.g., partner/colleague). | Prompt student to weigh options aloud, reformulate them clearly, and then produce a concise mediated summary: the final decision plus the main “if not” alternative. |
| Write / Synthesize (genre-aware output) | 3 min | Student writes a 150–180 word internal note or message to someone affected (roommate, colleague) explaining the change, the main consequence, and the agreed solution, using at least one first, one second, and one third conditional. | Help them keep tone practical and cooperative, not overly emotional; ensure conditionals are anchored in context. |
| Upgrade Feedback & Carry-Forward | 2 min | Teacher selects 2–3 sentences and upgrades them with more natural conditional phrasing and markers (“If we’d started earlier, we wouldn’t have had to rush; however, given that…, we’ll…”). Student compares and repeats. | Emphasize clarity of scenario–consequence link and pragmatic tone. |

## Lesson 5: Synthesize and Resolve – Daily Issues Brief for a Third Party

| Part | Plan |
|------|------|
| Time | 25 minutes |
| Main aim | The student can mediate between two short sources about daily-life issues and produce a 150–180 word genre-aware summary/resolution brief for a third party. |
| Materials | short “complaint-style” message + short “response or explanation” text, resolution-brief template, discourse marker card set |

| Stage | Time | Online material / activity | Teacher role |
|-------|------|----------------------------|--------------|
| Scenario Briefing | 2 min | Scenario: “Two people have had a minor issue (noise, schedule, misunderstanding). You are the person in the middle: you need to explain the situation to someone else (landlord, small business owner, friend) and propose a fair solution.” | Set up roles and stakes; clarify who the final reader is and what decision they can make. |
| Read (main source, sometimes paired with a short second source) | 6 min | Student reads a short message of complaint/request and a short reply or explanation from the other side. Each uses some present perfect (“this has been happening for…”) and narrative past. | Guide student to extract core facts, each person’s main concern, and points of agreement/disagreement. |
| Discourse & Language Focus (register and cohesion) | 5 min | Student identifies and labels discourse markers that structure a mediation-style text (“On the one hand… On the other hand… As a result… Overall…”). They also notice register (neutral, non-emotional language, indirect reference to both sides). | Explain how a mediation brief differs from a complaint: neutral reporting, balanced markers, and careful pronoun/reference choices. |
| Negotiate / Mediate (propose, negotiate, or summarize positions for a third party) | 7 min | Student orally mediates: in 90–120 seconds they must explain to the third party what has happened, what each side wants, and propose a practical resolution (“Given that… and whereas…, I’d recommend…”). Teacher can ask clarification questions as the third party. | Push for clear structure: situation → Side A → Side B → proposal. Model “bridging” phrases and help the student rephrase emotional language into neutral terms. |
| Write / Synthesize (genre-aware output) | 3 min | Student writes a 150–180 word mediation brief or “situation summary + recommendation” note for the third party, using balanced discourse markers and at least one conditional recommendation (“If we… then…”). | Support concise structure (3–4 short paragraphs/sections) and appropriate register (no direct blame). |
| Upgrade Feedback & Carry-Forward | 2 min | Teacher presents a short section “as written” and an upgraded version with better linking and more neutral tone. Student compares, repeats, and notes one “mediation frame” to use in future units (“Both parties agree that… However…”). | Emphasize transferable mediation structure and language as a key B2 tool. |

# Unit 2: Work and Study Life

## Lesson 1: Work and Study – Status Update to a Manager or Tutor

| Part | Plan |
|------|------|
| Time | 25 minutes |
| Main aim | The student can give a concise, structured update on work or study progress using present perfect and present perfect continuous in a semi-formal, professional register. |
| Materials | sample progress update email to manager/tutor, short project tracking note, progress-phrase bank |

| Stage | Time | Online material / activity | Teacher role |
|-------|------|----------------------------|--------------|
| Scenario Briefing | 2 min | Scenario: “You need to update your manager/tutor on what you’ve done recently, what you’ve been working on, and what still needs attention.” | Agree on a realistic project or course task; clarify expected formality and any deliverables. |

| Upgrade Feedback & Carry-Forward | 2 min | Teacher upgrades key lines, focusing on register (e.g., “I’ve done everything” → “I’ve completed the main tasks for this phase, namely…”), aspect, and linking.
