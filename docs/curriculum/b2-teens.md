# B2 Teens Curriculum

All 12 units of the B2 Teens track, each broken down lesson by lesson.
Every lesson is 25 minutes. Continues directly from B1 Teens — same 12
unit topics, deepened into issue-based discussion and identity-driven
content, per the B2 pedagogical pile's "intellectual and social
identity" driver for this age group.

Lesson shape (managed discussion and mediation, genuinely different
from B1 Teens' task-chain shape): **Stance Warm-up (2 min) → Read &
Notice, input text with contrasting views (6 min) → Discourse Focus,
cohesion and stance markers (4 min) → Debate & Mediate, managed
discussion comparing viewpoints and finding common ground (8 min) →
Produce, campaign-style or reflective output (3 min) → Upgrade
Feedback & Carry-Forward (2 min)**.

Grammar jump: past simple/continuous/perfect for layered reflective
narrative, present perfect and present perfect continuous for duration
and change, full modal nuance (could, might, would, should, must, may,
have to, ought to), first and second conditional with high control
plus selective third conditional for reflection and regret, relative
clauses and subordination for cause/concession/purpose/contrast, and
discourse markers used in real debate rather than essay drilling
(however, in contrast, on the other hand, as a result, overall). No B1
undershoot found in QA — teens are given the fuller grammar range per
the source doc's "teens can handle more abstraction than kids"
guidance.

**Correction made before saving:** Unit 7 was supposed to be "Town Life
and Getting Around" (the same topic as B1 Teens Unit 7, deepened), but
the draft substituted an off-spec duplicate unit ("Friends, Groups, and
Belonging" — nearly the same theme as Unit 2) and, as a side effect,
Units 8 and 9 (Travel and Holidays / Health and Fitness) ended up
swapped relative to the intended order. Fixed by writing a new Unit 7
on Town Life and Getting Around in the same voice/format/grammar
pattern as the rest of the file (mediation task, upgrade-feedback
stage, emotionally-safe framing around independence and curfews), and
swapping Units 8 and 9 back into the correct order. All other 11 units
checked out clean: correct topics, correct timing (2/6/4/8/3/2=25 on
every lesson), correct grammar scope, genuine mediation task in every
unit, 120–160 word writing targets present throughout.

Output targets: 120–160 word writing per lesson (up from B1 Teens'
80–120), 90–120 second speaking turns for debate/mediation (up from
B1 Teens' 60–90 seconds; shorter 60–90 second retells are used
specifically for the narrative/Lesson-2 storytelling task in each
unit, which is intentionally shorter than the debate task).

This is content/planning-stage only — none of it is built into the app
yet (no Supabase rows, no slide-type mapping, no images/audio).

# Unit 1: Identity and Personal Style

## Lesson 1: Backstory of Style and Identity

| Part | Plan |
|------|------|
| Time | 25 minutes |
| Main aim | The student can describe how their personal style and identity have changed over time using present perfect and present perfect continuous in a nuanced way. |
| Materials | stance slider statements, 3-paragraph reflective blog post with comments, highlighter tool, timeline/identity-note template |

| Stage | Time | Online material / activity | Teacher role |
|-------|------|----------------------------|--------------|
| Stance Warm-up | 2 min | Student reacts quickly on a stance slider (strongly agree → strongly disagree) to two prompts: "My style shows who I really am" and "People online have changed how I see myself," giving one short reason each. | Keep it light and safe, monitor emotional reactions, and surface initial views without pushing. |
| Read & Notice (input text with contrasting views) | 6 min | Student reads a 3-paragraph reflective blog post where a teen explains how their style has changed: they have always liked comfortable clothes but have been experimenting with makeup and new looks; a friend comment and social media have slowly changed how they see themselves. Two short comments under the post show a supportive view and a more critical view about "trying too hard." | Guide gist first, then help the student identify what has changed for the writer and what has stayed the same, plus the basic stance of each commenter. |
| Discourse Focus (cohesion and stance markers) | 4 min | Student highlights present perfect/continuous forms ("have always felt," "have been trying," "has changed") and stance markers like "to be honest," "in a way," "on the other hand." They connect each to either "past experience," "change in progress," or "showing a balanced opinion." | Briefly clarify form/meaning in context and draw attention to how the writer softens or balances their opinion, not just the grammar itself. |
| Debate & Mediate (managed discussion, compare viewpoints, find common ground) | 8 min | Student first summarizes the blogger's position in 2–3 sentences, then compares the two commenters: Who is more understanding? Where do they agree or disagree about "changing yourself"? Teacher prompts with questions like "What part do they see in a similar way?" and "If you were the blogger, what would you say back?" | Manage turn-taking in a one-to-one setting, gently push for 90–120 second stretches where the student explains and compares views, and keep the tone supportive around self-image. |
| Produce (campaign-style or reflective output) | 3 min | Student writes a 120–150 word reflective note titled "How my style has changed" using at least two present perfect/continuous forms and one stance marker (e.g., "Honestly," "In my opinion"). | Support quick planning, suggest one extra detail where needed, and remind the student to show both experience and change. |
| Upgrade Feedback & Carry-Forward | 2 min | Teacher reads back a short part of the student's note in their original version, then offers a more B2-like upgrade (e.g., "I changed my style" → "My style has changed a lot over the last two years, especially since..."). Student chooses one upgrade to keep using next time. | Focus feedback on upgrading range and nuance (aspect, stance markers), not just fixing errors, and set a clear micro-goal for the next lesson. |

## Lesson 2: Story Behind the Outfit

| Part | Plan |
|------|------|
| Time | 25 minutes |
| Main aim | The student can tell and analyze a layered narrative about a style or identity moment using past simple, past continuous, and past perfect. |
| Materials | prompt images of a school event and casual day, 3-paragraph narrative blog post, event timeline template, narrative language cards |

| Stage | Time | Online material / activity | Teacher role |
|-------|------|----------------------------|--------------|
| Stance Warm-up | 2 min | Student chooses which is more stressful for them: "non-uniform day" or "class photo day," and briefly explains why. | Normalize a range of feelings about appearance and public moments; keep it non-judgmental. |
| Read & Notice (input text with contrasting views) | 6 min | Student reads a 3-paragraph narrative where a teen describes a non-uniform day: what they had planned to wear, what actually happened when they arrived, and how they felt later. A short friend message at the end shows a different interpretation of the same outfit ("trying too hard" vs "brave and cool"). | Guide the student to track the sequence of events and the different interpretations of the same moment. |
| Discourse Focus (cohesion and stance markers) | 4 min | Student underlines past simple, past continuous, and past perfect ("I had spent hours choosing," "I was walking into school when..."), then highlights discourse markers like "at first," "while," "by the time," "in the end." They map these onto a simple line from "before the day" to "after the day." | Help the student see how tense and markers work together to control perspective and reflection. |
| Debate & Mediate (managed discussion, compare viewpoints, find common ground) | 8 min | Student retells the story in 60–90 seconds, then explains how the friend in the message sees it differently. Together, they explore: Where do the two views clash? Where could they agree (e.g., it was a big deal, it took courage)? Student practices phrasing like "From their point of view..." and "I can see why, but..." | Scaffold mediation language, prompt gentle empathy, and model ways to talk about disagreement without attacking the friend. |
| Produce (campaign-style or reflective output) | 3 min | Student writes 120–160 words titled "The story behind one outfit" about a real or imagined moment, using a mixture of past simple, past continuous, and at least one past perfect sentence. | Support quick planning (3 key moments), encourage one reflective sentence about what they learned from that moment. |
| Upgrade Feedback & Carry-Forward | 2 min | Teacher selects a short piece of the student's narrative and upgrades it by tightening time references and adding one stance marker (e.g., "Later, I realized..."). Student repeats the upgraded version aloud. | Emphasize narrative control and reflective language; set a micro-goal like "use at least one 'by the time' or 'in the end' sentence next time." |

## Lesson 3: Pressure, Choice, and Style Rules

| Part | Plan |
|------|------|
| Time | 25 minutes |
| Main aim | The student can express nuanced opinions about style pressure using modal verbs and stance discourse markers, while recognizing other teens' perspectives. |
| Materials | agree/disagree slider statements, 3-paragraph opinion article with two contrasting teen quotes, modal/stance phrase bank, opinion-notes template |

| Stage | Time | Online material / activity | Teacher role |
|-------|------|----------------------------|--------------|
| Stance Warm-up | 2 min | Student reacts to two quick statements: "Schools should decide what we wear" and "People must look perfect online" using an agree–disagree slider and one short reason per statement. | Keep tone exploratory, not confrontational; acknowledge feelings about pressure. |
| Read & Notice (input text with contrasting views) | 6 min | Student reads a 3-paragraph opinion piece: the writer argues that there should be more freedom at school but also some basic style rules "so that people feel safe and respected." Two embedded quotes from other teens show a stricter view ("students must follow rules") and a more relaxed one ("people should be able to experiment"). | Guide reading for the main argument and the range of opinions; highlight that there is more than a simple "for/against" split. |
| Discourse Focus (cohesion and stance markers) | 4 min | Student highlights modal verbs (should, must, have to, might, could, ought to) and stance markers such as "to be honest," "in my view," "on the other hand," "overall." They match each to a function: strong rule, recommendation, possibility, softening, contrast, or conclusion. | Clarify how the writer keeps a balanced tone while still making clear points. |
| Debate & Mediate (managed discussion, compare viewpoints, find common ground) | 8 min | Teacher sets up a mini-managed discussion: the student first summarizes the writer's view, then chooses which of the two quoted teens they are closer to and explains why. Next, they try to find a compromise rule set (e.g., "Students should be able to..., but they have to..."). | Keep the discussion structured; nudge the student to use modals and stance markers to negotiate a middle ground, highlighting emotional safety around rules and self-expression. |
| Produce (campaign-style or reflective output) | 3 min | Student writes a 120–160 word mini-manifesto titled "Style rules that feel fair" using at least three different modals and two stance markers. | Support quick idea organization (3–4 short points), encourage one sentence acknowledging other people's feelings. |
| Upgrade Feedback & Carry-Forward | 2 min | Teacher chooses one or two sentences from the manifesto and upgrades them by adjusting modality (e.g., "students can" → "students should be able to") and adding a contrast marker ("However..."). Student repeats or rewrites the upgraded version. | Focus feedback on nuance in obligation/choice and the use of discourse markers in argument. |

## Lesson 4: What If I Had Chosen Differently?

| Part | Plan |
|------|------|
| Time | 25 minutes |
| Main aim | The student can explore hypothetical and regret-based identity choices using first, second, and selective third conditional structures. |
| Materials | fork-in-the-road style scenarios, 3-paragraph reflective narrative with hypothetical comments, conditional pattern cards, decision-map template |

| Stage | Time | Online material / activity | Teacher role |
|-------|------|----------------------------|--------------|
| Stance Warm-up | 2 min | Student chooses which feels more powerful: changing their look or changing their online profile, and says why. | Keep it reflective; emphasize that there is no "right" answer. |
| Read & Notice (input text with contrasting views) | 6 min | Student reads a 3-paragraph narrative in which a teen describes a big style/identity decision (e.g., cutting their hair, changing how they show themselves online). The text includes first conditional for future consequences, second conditional for imagined alternative selves, and one or two third conditional regrets ("If I hadn't posted that picture, people wouldn't have..."), plus a friend comment offering an alternative, kinder interpretation. | Guide the student to follow the emotional journey and notice where the writer imagines different outcomes or regrets. |
| Discourse Focus (cohesion and stance markers) | 4 min | Student highlights examples of first, second, and third conditionals and matches them to labels: real future, imaginary now, and past regret. They also underline stance markers like "in the end," "looking back," "to be honest," and connect them to reflection. | Clarify patterns without over-technical talk; keep grammar and discourse linked to self-reflection. |
| Debate & Mediate (managed discussion, compare viewpoints, find common ground) | 8 min | Student explains the writer's main regret in their own words, then summarizes the friend's more positive view. They discuss questions like: "Where do they actually agree?" and "If you were giving advice to the writer now, what would you say they could still do?" using conditional prompts. | Manage the emotional load carefully, encouraging empathy and framing regrets as learning, not self-attack. |
| Produce (campaign-style or reflective output) | 3 min | Student writes 120–160 words titled "If I had chosen differently..." about a real or imagined moment, including at least one second conditional and one third conditional sentence, plus one line about what they can still do now. | Help with safe topic boundaries if needed; encourage focus on learning and future action rather than blame. |
| Upgrade Feedback & Carry-Forward | 2 min | Teacher selects a short part of the student's text and upgrades conditional range and discourse markers (e.g., "If I didn't" → "If I hadn't"; adding "Looking back" at the start). Student practices the upgraded version aloud. | Emphasize how upgraded language makes reflection clearer and more B2-like, not more "dramatic." |

## Lesson 5: Identity Roundtable – Mediate and Showcase

| Part | Plan |
|------|------|
| Time | 25 minutes |
| Main aim | The student can compare and mediate between two different views on identity and style, then produce a short showcase piece summarizing their own position. |
| Materials | summary cards of previous texts, new 3-paragraph mini-roundtable article with two teen profiles, mediation prompt sheet, campaign/reflective output frame |

| Stage | Time | Online material / activity | Teacher role |
|-------|------|----------------------------|--------------|
| Stance Warm-up | 2 min | Student quickly reviews three short statements from earlier in the unit (e.g., "Style shows the real you"; "Rules help people feel safe"; "Online images change how we see ourselves") and marks any stance that has changed for them since Lesson 1. | Encourage noticing of change in perspective; validate shifts or mixed feelings. |
| Read & Notice (input text with contrasting views) | 6 min | Student reads a 3-paragraph "roundtable" article featuring two short teen profiles: one who believes style is mainly for fun and community, another who sees style as serious self-expression and sometimes pressure. The article includes how their views have changed over time and a short paragraph from a school counselor trying to connect the two perspectives. | Guide reading for where each person stands, how their views have developed, and where the counselor sees overlap. |
| Discourse Focus (cohesion and stance markers) | 4 min | Student highlights present perfect for change ("has started to feel"), modals and stance markers ("might be", "on the other hand", "overall"), and mediation phrases used by the counselor ("both of them agree that...", "where they differ is..."). | Draw attention to language that connects viewpoints and marks agreement/disagreement clearly. |
| Debate & Mediate (managed discussion, compare viewpoints, find common ground) | 8 min | Student plays the role of "mediator friend": first they summarize Teen A's view to Teen B (in 3–4 sentences), then Teen B's view to Teen A, using prompts like "From your point of view..." and "What you both seem to want is..." They then state where they see common ground and one real difference that might stay. | Structure the mediation carefully, model phrases when needed, and keep tone respectful; encourage 90–120 second stretches focusing on explanation rather than winning. |
| Produce (campaign-style or reflective output) | 3 min | Student writes a 120–160 word mini-piece titled "Being yourself with or without style" – either as a short blog post or a campaign caption – summarizing their current view and mentioning at least one idea from another teen in the unit. | Support quick structure (situation → views → their position), encourage naming one thing they respect in the other position. |
| Upgrade Feedback & Carry-Forward | 2 min | Teacher takes one short part of the student’s piece explaining agreement/disagreement and upgrades it with more precise stance markers and modals (e.g., "I don't like" → "Overall, I feel uncomfortable when...", "people must" → "people probably should"). Student repeats the improved version and notes one phrase they want to keep using in the next unit. | Focus on making disagreement sound thoughtful and B2-like, not aggressive; set a carry-forward goal about mediation language for Unit 2. |

# Unit 2: Friends and Social Life

## Lesson 1: Backstory of Friends and Social Circles

| Part | Plan |
|------|------|
| Time | 25 minutes |
| Main aim | The student can describe how their friendships and social circles have changed over time using present perfect and present perfect continuous, while recognizing different comfort levels in social life. |
| Materials | social-circle spectrum prompts, 3-paragraph reflective blog post with contrasting comments, highlighter tool, social-history notes template |

| Stage | Time | Online material / activity | Teacher role |
|-------|------|----------------------------|--------------|
| Stance Warm-up | 2 min | Student places themselves on a quick spectrum for two prompts: "I like having a big group of friends" and "I prefer a few close friends," giving one short reason for each choice. | Normalize different social styles, keep tone non-judgmental, and avoid pressuring the student to disclose sensitive details. |
| Read & Notice (input text with contrasting views) | 6 min | Student reads a 3-paragraph reflective blog where a teen explains how their social life has changed: they have always been shy but have been chatting more in clubs recently; their circle has slowly grown, but they still feel awkward in big groups. Two short comments below show a very outgoing teen saying "just talk to everyone" and another shy teen saying "online chats have helped a lot." | Guide reading for what has changed, what has stayed the same, and how the commenters’ experiences differ yet connect to the writer’s. |
| Discourse Focus (cohesion and stance markers) | 4 min | Student highlights present perfect/continuous ("have always been shy," "has changed," "have been joining") and stance markers like "to be honest," "in some ways," "on the other hand." They sort them under headings: long-term pattern, recent change, and balanced opinion. | Clarify how aspect and stance language build a nuanced picture of social change, not a simple "now I’m confident" story. |
| Debate & Mediate (managed discussion, compare viewpoints, find common ground) | 8 min | Student first summarizes the blogger’s position, then compares the two commenters: Who sounds more similar to the writer? Where do all three agree (e.g., making friends takes time)? Where do they disagree about "just talking more" as a solution? | Manage a calm, reflective discussion; prompt mediation phrases like "X feels that..., while Y thinks..., but they both agree that...", keeping emotional safety around social anxiety. |
| Produce (campaign-style or reflective output) | 3 min | Student writes 120–160 words titled "How my social life has changed" (or an imagined version), using at least two present perfect/continuous forms and two stance markers. | Support idea ordering (past → now → small future hope), suggest one sentence that acknowledges different comfort levels in groups. |
| Upgrade Feedback & Carry-Forward | 2 min | Teacher selects 2–3 sentences and upgrades them by refining aspect and stance (e.g., "I changed a lot" → "My social life has changed slowly over the last two years, especially since..."). Student repeats the upgraded version aloud and notes one phrase to reuse. | Focus feedback on more precise time framing and balanced stance, not just grammar correction. |

## Lesson 2: The Story of a Falling-Out and a Fix

| Upgrade Feedback & Carry-Forward | 2 min | Teacher upgrades one short section by tightening the timeline and adding a stance marker ("At first... but later...") and a more neutral mediation phrase ("I can understand why they were upset"). # Unit 3: School and Study Skills

## Lesson 1: Backstory of School and Study Habits

| Part | Plan |
|------|------|
| Time | 25 minutes |
| Main aim | The student can describe how their study habits and feelings about school have changed over time using present perfect and present perfect continuous, linking habits to identity rather than just marks. |
| Materials | school-attitude spectrum prompts, 3-paragraph reflective blog post with contrasting comments, highlighter tool, study-history notes template |

| Stage | Time | Online material / activity | Teacher role |
|-------|------|----------------------------|--------------|
