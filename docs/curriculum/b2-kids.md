# B2 Kids Curriculum

All 12 units of the B2 Kids track, each broken down lesson by lesson.
Every lesson is 25 minutes. Continues directly from B1 Kids — same 12
unit topics, reframed as bigger creative missions/projects rather than
new topics, per the B2 pedagogical pile's "creative agency" driver for
this age group.

Lesson shape (mission/project arc, genuinely different from B1 Kids'
shape): **Mission Warm-up (3 min) → Read / Story Input (6 min) →
Language Noticing, from the text (5 min) → Build the Mission, guided
speaking or writing (6 min) → Present / Show and Tell (3 min) →
Reflect & Carry Forward (2 min)**.

Grammar scope (deliberately trimmed from general B2 for this age
group): present perfect for experience/change over time, past simple +
past continuous with one controlled past perfect twist per story
lesson, modals limited to should/must/have to/could/might (no "may" or
"ought to" — too formal for this age), first and second conditional
only (no third conditional — too abstract), simple relative clauses
and kid-friendly connectors (first, next, after that, in the end,
however, for example) rather than formal academic discourse markers.
No B2-level overreach found in QA.

Output targets: 70–100 word guided writing per lesson (up from B1
Kids' 4–6 sentences), 60–90 second speaking turns (up from B1 Kids'
30–45 seconds). Every unit includes at least one light mediation task
(explaining a rule to a younger child, comparing two characters'
choices, recommending an option) alongside the main Lesson 5
comparison/recommendation task.

This is content/planning-stage only — none of it is built into the app
yet (no Supabase rows, no slide-type mapping, no images/audio).

# Unit 1: School Life and School Projects

## Lesson 1: The Mystery Project Board

| Part | Plan |
|------|------|
| Time | 25 minutes |
| Main aim | The student can talk and write about school project experiences using present perfect and topic vocabulary. |
| Materials | mission board image, 3-paragraph story text, project word cards, writing frame |

| Stage | Time | Online material / activity | Teacher role |
|-------|------|----------------------------|--------------|
| Mission Warm-up | 3 min | Student opens a “mystery project board” with pictures of a robot poster, a class garden, a recycled model, and a school play sign, then answers: Which project looks most exciting? Which one have you already tried? | Set the mission hook, activate school project vocabulary, and prompt short experience-based answers. |
| Read / Story Input | 6 min | Student reads a 3-paragraph story about two children who find an old school challenge board and discover that their class has already finished one project but has never tried the biggest challenge: building a “kindness maze” for younger students. Student matches each paragraph to one mission step. | Guide the student through gist first, then help find experience language and key project details. |
| Language Noticing (from the text) | 5 min | Student highlights present perfect forms such as “have finished,” “has already made,” and “have never built,” then sorts them into experience, finished action, and change over time. | Draw attention to meaning inside the mission story and keep grammar tied to the project context. |
| Build the Mission (guided speaking or writing) | 6 min | Student completes a project planner: “Our class has already… / We haven’t… yet / I’ve never… before / We’ve learned that…” and writes 70–90 words about a dream school project team. | Support sentence building, prompt fuller ideas, and recycle the unit vocabulary naturally. |
| Present / Show and Tell | 3 min | Student gives a 60–90 second mini presentation about their project team and says which challenge the team should try first. | Encourage clear speaking, help with one extra detail, and note useful language for praise. |
| Reflect & Carry Forward | 2 min | Student chooses one new project job they would like next lesson and says why. | Close the mission, review language, and set up the next story step. |

## Lesson 2: The Project Room Surprise

| Part | Plan |
|------|------|
| Time | 25 minutes |
| Main aim | The student can retell a school project story with a twist using past simple, past continuous, and one controlled past perfect moment. |
| Materials | story slides, 4-paragraph narrative text, timeline cards, retelling frame |

| Stage | Time | Online material / activity | Teacher role |
|-------|------|----------------------------|--------------|
| Mission Warm-up | 3 min | Student looks at three clues from a school project room — a broken model, wet paint, and a missing notebook — and guesses what happened. | Build suspense, activate project-story vocabulary, and invite prediction. |
| Read / Story Input | 6 min | Student reads a 4-paragraph story about children who were preparing a class display when they found that someone had moved the project box. While they were looking under the tables, they discovered that the art teacher had already taken the best poster to the hall. Student orders four event cards. | Guide the learner through story sequence and help notice the twist moment clearly. |
| Language Noticing (from the text) | 5 min | Student underlines past simple actions, past continuous background actions, and the one past perfect twist sentence, then labels them on a timeline with first, next, after that, and in the end. | Keep the grammar embedded in the narrative and show how the tenses build the surprise. |
| Build the Mission (guided speaking or writing) | 6 min | Student retells the project-room mystery with a guided storyboard and then writes 70–100 words about what happened, including one background action and one twist line. | Support accurate sequencing and help the student stretch the story with detail. |
| Present / Show and Tell | 3 min | Student gives a 60–90 second dramatic retelling of the mystery and explains which clue mattered most. | Prompt expressive retelling and quick self-correction if needed. |
| Reflect & Carry Forward | 2 min | Student says what a good project team should do next time to avoid the same problem. | Link the story to the next mission stage and preview advice language. |

| Reflect & Carry Forward | 2 min | Student chooses one rule they will remember for the next mission and says why. | Consolidate practical language and prepare the “what if” lesson. |

## Lesson 2: The Missing Plan Notebook

| Part | Plan |
|------|------|
| Time | 25 minutes |
| Main aim | The student can retell a home mission story using past simple, past continuous, and one controlled past perfect twist. |
| Materials | notebook clues, 4-paragraph story, event cards, mission notebook |

| Stage | Time | Online material / activity | Teacher role |
|-------|------|----------------------------|--------------|
| Mission Warm-up | 3 min | Student looks at clues — a pencil under the table, a watering can by the door, and a half-open drawer — and guesses what happened to the team’s home project notebook. | Build suspense and reactivate home-help vocabulary. |
| Read / Story Input | 6 min | Student reads a 4-paragraph story about children who were preparing a home-help fair when their plan notebook disappeared. While they were searching in the kitchen and hallway, they discovered that a parent had already moved the notebook to the shelf beside the project boxes. | Guide the story sequence and help the learner notice the twist clearly. |
| Language Noticing (from the text) | 5 min | Student marks main actions, background actions, and the one past perfect sentence, then uses first, next, after that, and in the end to rebuild the story. | Keep grammar inside the mission story and support timeline control. |
| Build the Mission (guided speaking or writing) | 6 min | Student fills in a project detective notebook and writes 70–100 words retelling the missing-notebook story with one background action and one twist line. | Support fuller retelling and encourage one feeling or reaction sentence. |
| Present / Show and Tell | 3 min | Student gives a 60–90 second dramatic retell and explains which clue helped most. | Encourage expressive storytelling and clear sequence. |
| Reflect & Carry Forward | 2 min | Student says what a home-helper team should check before starting a project. | Prepare naturally for the advice mission. |

## Lesson 3: The Home Helper Guide

| Part | Plan |
|------|------|
| Time | 25 minutes |
| Main aim | The student can give advice and instructions about helping at home using modals in a practical mission guide. |
| Materials | home problem cards, 3-paragraph guide text, modal chart, helper poster frame |

| Stage | Time | Online material / activity | Teacher role |
|-------|------|----------------------------|--------------|
| Mission Warm-up | 3 min | Student chooses a home-help problem card: forgotten job, messy room, no time, or too many tasks at once. | Activate practical helping-at-home problems and prepare advice language. |
| Read / Story Input | 6 min | Student reads a 3-paragraph “Home Helper Guide” for children planning jobs at home. It explains how to organize tasks, ask for help, share jobs, and support family plans. | Guide reading for useful rules and practical help ideas. |
| Language Noticing (from the text) | 5 min | Student finds should, must, have to, could, and might, then matches them to strong rule, helpful idea, or possible action. | Keep the modal work practical and linked to the mission. |

| Present / Show and Tell | 3 min | Student presents the top three home-helper rules and explains one useful tip. | Encourage practical explanation and confident modal use. |
| Reflect & Carry Forward | 2 min | Student chooses one home-helper rule they would really use this week. | Reinforce learning and preview conditionals. |

## Lesson 4: What If the Future Plan Changes?

| Part | Plan |
|------|------|
| Time | 25 minutes |
| Main aim | The student can explore likely and imaginary home or future mission situations using first and second conditionals. |
| Materials | future-change cards, 3-paragraph mission brief, if-cards, home-plan sheet |

| Stage | Time | Online material / activity | Teacher role |
|-------|------|----------------------------|--------------|
| Mission Warm-up | 3 min | Student chooses one future-plan surprise: visitors arrive early, a job takes longer, a pet makes a mess, or a magic helper robot appears. | Spark playful “what if” thinking with concrete home situations. |
| Read / Story Input | 6 min | Student reads a 3-paragraph mission brief about a home-helper project with real problems and dream changes. The team must decide what they will do if the plan changes and what they would do if they had a magic helper for one day. | Help the student separate likely problems from imaginative ideas. |
| Language Noticing (from the text) | 5 min | Student highlights first conditional rescue plans and second conditional dream ideas, then sorts them into “real home plan” and “magic home plan.” | Clarify meaning through mission categories and examples. |
| Build the Mission (guided speaking or writing) | 6 min | Student completes a home rescue planner and writes 70–100 words with at least one first conditional and one second conditional idea. | Support accurate if-sentences and encourage clear, creative solutions. |
| Present / Show and Tell | 3 min | Student presents one real home rescue plan and one magical future idea. | Encourage contrast and simple explanation. |
| Reflect & Carry Forward | 2 min | Student says which plan could really help at home. | Prepare for the final mission review and recommendation. |

## Lesson 5: Mission Complete – The Future Helper Showcase

| Part | Plan |
|------|------|
| Time | 25 minutes |
| Main aim | The student can review the home-help mission, retell key events, and recommend the best project or future plan in a final mediation-style task. |
| Materials | showcase board, review text, compare-the-plans chart, final talk frame |

| Stage | Time | Online material / activity | Teacher role |
|-------|------|----------------------------|--------------|
| Mission Warm-up | 3 min | Student enters the “Future Helper Showcase” and chooses the most important mission moment from the week. | Reactivate the final unit story and build showcase energy. |
| Read / Story Input | 6 min | Student reads a 3-paragraph showcase guide that retells the home-helper mission and presents two final home project plans created by different teams. | Guide reading for summary and comparison. |
| Language Noticing (from the text) | 5 min | Student finds one example from each grammar focus in the unit and explains what each one helps the writer do. | Turn review into active and meaningful noticing. |
| Build the Mission (guided speaking or writing) | 6 min | Student fills in a comparison chart and writes 80–100 words recommending one future home project plan to a younger helper. | Support summary, comparison, and simple recommendation language. |
| Present / Show and Tell | 3 min | Student gives a 60–90 second showcase talk summarizing the mission and recommending one plan. | Encourage confidence, clear speaking, and a final opinion with reasons. |
| Reflect & Carry Forward | 2 min | Student says one home or future skill they feel proud of now. | Close the final unit with reflection and achievement. |
