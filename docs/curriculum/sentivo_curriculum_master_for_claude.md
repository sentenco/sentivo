# Sentivo Curriculum Design Notes for Claude

This document combines the current working guidance for Sentivo’s curriculum structure across CEFR levels, lesson progression, and slide-based lesson design for online one-to-one classes.

---

# Intra-Level Progression Framework

Sentivo’s current issue is not level-to-level progression, but **within-level flatness**: units change topic, but difficulty remains almost static inside a CEFR level.[web:1619][web:1799] A stronger design is to keep units self-contained while introducing a **deliberate intra-level progression grid** that gradually increases complexity across all 12 units.[web:1800][web:1804]

## Core recommendation

The current four proposed levers are valid and align with CEFR-aware curriculum design:

- **Grammar and structure complexity**: move from one target in isolation to combining 2–3 targets in connected sentences and short texts.[web:1803][web:1619]
- **Text length and density**: increase from short disconnected sentences to coherent paragraphs and, at higher levels, multi-paragraph texts.[web:1795]
- **Vocabulary sophistication**: move from concrete, high-frequency words to more abstract, opinion-based, and discourse-level vocabulary while staying inside the level ceiling.[web:1799]
- **Task independence**: reduce scaffolding over time, moving from word banks and sentence frames to freer, self-generated production.[web:1800][web:1804]

Two additional levers should be added for a stronger progression model:

- **Cognitive and discourse complexity**: move from naming and describing to comparing, justifying, problem-solving, and expressing viewpoints.[web:1793][web:1799]
- **Skill integration and modality**: move from isolated skill practice to chained tasks such as read→speak, listen→write, or compare→discuss→write.[web:1802]

Together, these six levers form a consistent intra-level progression system that can be reused across Kids, Teens, Adults, and all CEFR levels.[web:1805][web:1619]

## Recommended 12-unit structure

A strong structure is to group the 12 units of each CEFR level into **three tiers of four units** rather than treat progression as 12 tiny equal steps.[web:1716][web:1722]

- **Units 1–4: Foundation**
- **Units 5–8: Combination / Expansion**
- **Units 9–12: Near-mastery / Bridge to next level**

This keeps progression visible without making individual units too dependent on strict chronological order.[web:1800][web:1719]

## Tier progression grid

| Lever | Units 1–4: Foundation | Units 5–8: Combination | Units 9–12: Bridge |
|---|---|---|---|
| Grammar / structure | One target at a time; short simple sentences | Combine 2 targets in one exchange or sentence | Combine 2–3 targets across a paragraph or extended dialogue |
| Text length / density | 3–5 short sentences; low cohesion | 6–8 sentences; coherent mini-paragraph | 8–12+ sentences; more connected, denser text |
| Vocabulary | Concrete, high-frequency, topic-basic | More adjectives, adverbs, collocations, opinion words | More abstract/topic nuance, still within CEFR ceiling |
| Task independence | Heavy scaffolds; copying, matching, gap-fills | Partial scaffolds; guided prompts | Minimal scaffolds; prompts/checklists only |
| Cognitive complexity | Naming, identifying, describing | Comparing, giving reasons, simple advice | Justifying, weighing options, explaining consequences |
| Skill integration | Mostly isolated skills | Some skill chaining | Regular integrated tasks |

This grid should be constant across the platform; only the actual language load changes by CEFR band.[web:1619][web:1803]

## Age-track adjustments

The same progression logic can be used across Kids, Teens, and Adults, but the **slope and mode** should differ.

### Kids (approx. ages 6–12)

Kids need the **gentlest progression** inside a level, with more recycling and more visual/oral support.[web:1793] Text growth should be modest, scaffolding should remain visible for longer, and later-tier difficulty should come more from **slightly longer routines and richer combinations** than from abstraction.[web:1804]

Recommended adjustments:

- Smaller jumps between tiers.
- More repetition of lesson pattern.
- More picture support, chants, stories, and oral response.
- Free production remains relatively controlled even in Units 9–12.

### Teens

Teens can handle a **moderate progression slope** and benefit from increased reasoning, comparison, and opinion work inside a level.[web:1793] Later units can use more integrated skills and more open tasks, especially around school, media, identity, and social topics.

Recommended adjustments:

- Moderate jump between tiers.
- More self-generated speaking/writing from Units 5–8 onward.
- Stronger movement toward opinion, viewpoint, and comparison tasks.

### Adults

Adults can usually handle the **steepest within-level progression**, especially in text density, abstraction, and skill integration.[web:1794] Even at A2/B1, later units can include more authentic text types such as emails, schedules, messages, announcements, and brief reports, as long as the language stays within CEFR limits.

Recommended adjustments:

- Steeper tier jumps.
- Earlier integration of reading, speaking, and writing.
- More real-world task types and communicative goals.

## How this generalizes across A1–C2

### A1–B2

For A1–B2, keep the existing **fixed 9-part lesson architecture**, but make each tier heavier on the six progression levers.[web:1802][web:1805] That means Unit 1 and Unit 11 can keep the same lesson headings while differing in sentence complexity, text size, vocabulary load, amount of scaffolding, and cognitive demand.

### C1–C2

For C1/C2, keep the separate **Performance / Model / Output** architecture, but apply the same three-tier logic across the 12 units.[file:1527] The progression should not be based on “more grammar” so much as on:

- denser and less scaffolded input,
- longer and more integrated tasks,
- greater pragmatic control,
- more complex mediation and interaction,
- more independence in planning, stance, and performance.

A useful mapping is:

- **Tier 1: lower C-band / supported advanced work**
- **Tier 2: full descriptor-level C1 or C2 performance**
- **Tier 3: near-exam / near-real-world bridge tasks with minimal scaffolding**

This preserves consistency across the entire Sentivo curriculum even though the lesson shape changes at the advanced bands.[file:1527][web:1619]

## Risks and mitigation

### Students may skip around units

If units become too dependent on strict order, later-tier units may feel inaccessible.[web:1719] To avoid this:

- Keep each unit **self-contained**.
- Keep difficulty reasonably similar **within each 4-unit tier**.
- Label units internally by tier, even if learners do not see the label.
- Offer placement or recommendation logic that nudges learners toward the start of a tier if needed.

### Review lessons may become uneven

If all review lessons stay easy while later units get harder, pacing will feel inconsistent.[web:1797][web:1801] Review should exist at all three tier levels:

- Unit 4 review = foundation review
- Unit 8 review = combination review
- Unit 12 review = bridge review

### Teacher workload may rise

If progression is not transparent, teachers will experience the new structure as hidden inconsistency.[web:1738][web:1741] To reduce this:

- Build a one-page **progression matrix** per level.
- Show expected text length, grammar load, scaffolding level, and task openness by unit.
- Keep lesson templates visually consistent even when linguistic complexity increases.

### “Harder” can accidentally become “off-level”

Later-tier units should feel harder **within the same CEFR band**, not like the next level too early.[web:1619][web:1803] The safest way to do this is to increase **combination, density, and independence** more than to introduce truly next-level grammar prematurely.

## Practical framework for curriculum writers

For every CEFR level and every age track, create a planning sheet with these columns:

| Unit | Tier | Main topic | Grammar load | Text length target | Vocabulary type | Scaffolding level | Cognitive demand | Integration level |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Foundation | e.g. Hobbies | 1 target | 3–5 sentences | concrete | heavy | describe | isolated |
| 6 | Combination | e.g. Media | 2 targets combined | 6–8 sentences | opinion + concrete | medium | compare/reason | some integration |
| 11 | Bridge | e.g. Community | 2–3 targets integrated | 8–12+ sentences | more abstract | light | justify/explain | integrated |

## Concrete example: A2 Teens

### Unit 1 lesson example (Foundation tier)

- Grammar focus: **simple past** in isolation.
- Reading text: 4 short sentences about what a teen did last weekend.
- Vocabulary: concrete activities such as watch TV, play games, visit family.
- Tasks: matching, gap-fill, short sentence completion, very short supported speaking.
- Production support: heavy sentence frames such as “Yesterday I ___ with my ___.”

### Unit 8 lesson example (Combination tier)

- Grammar focus: **simple past + comparatives + because**.
- Reading text: 7–8 sentence paragraph comparing two weekends.
- Vocabulary: activity words plus opinion adjectives like boring, exciting, relaxing.
- Tasks: identify comparison language, discuss which weekend was better and why, write 5–6 connected sentences.
- Production support: partial prompts instead of full frames.

### Unit 12 lesson example (Bridge tier)

- Grammar focus: **simple past + comparatives + going to + connectors**.
- Reading text: 10–12 sentence connected text about past habits and future changes.
- Vocabulary: concrete plus slightly more abstract words such as routine, goal, active, improve.
- Tasks: track time contrast, explain changes, write a short connected paragraph using a checklist rather than sentence frames.
- Production support: minimal; mostly prompts and output criteria.

## Recommended final model for Sentivo

The strongest curriculum structure is:

- keep current lesson architectures (9-part for A1–B2, M/O/P for C1–C2),
- add a **three-tier, 12-unit progression model** inside every CEFR level,
- use six progression levers in every level,
- adjust the progression slope by age track,
- protect self-containment by making units interchangeable **within tiers**,
- build writer-facing matrices and tier-aware review points.

# Lesson Micro-Pattern for 25-Minute One-to-One Online Classes

The lesson-level pattern should be **structurally consistent but experientially varied**. Every class should have the same simple internal spine, but the activities inside that spine should rotate enough that students do not feel they are doing the same class again and again.[web:1836][web:1762]

For Sentivo, especially in **25-minute online one-to-one ESL classes**, the best approach is:

- keep a stable micro-structure,
- vary the activity types inside each stage,
- make every activity fast, interactive, and low-friction for online delivery,
- ensure all tasks work well with **one teacher and one student**, not group assumptions.[web:1826][web:1835]

## Core recommendation

Use one shared lesson spine for planning, but do **not** repeat the same exact task pattern every lesson.

A good default is:

- **Engage**
- **Study / Guided Practice**
- **Activate / Freer Use + Wrap-Up**

This can also be described as:

- warm-up and activation,
- focused language work,
- freer performance and recap.[web:1761][web:1758]

## Why not make every lesson totally different?

A fully different lesson pattern every time creates teacher confusion, makes training harder, and weakens progression tracking.[web:1742][web:1833] Learners usually benefit from a recognizable structure because they know what is expected and can focus on the language.[web:1820][web:1623]

## Why not make every lesson identical?

If the stages, activity types, prompts, and interaction moves are all repeated with only new vocabulary, students will feel the curriculum is flat and predictable.[web:1839][web:1834]

So the right answer is:

- **same backbone**,
- **different task experience**.

## Recommended micro-pattern for 25-minute 1:1 online classes

### Engage

Purpose:

- get the student talking quickly,
- connect emotionally or personally to the topic,
- activate prior language,
- establish the lesson goal.

Good 1:1 online activity types:

- quick preference question,
- picture prompt,
- either/or choice,
- mini poll,
- “tell me one thing about…” challenge,
- true/false prediction,
- quick memory question from last lesson.

### Study / Guided Practice

Purpose:

- focus on the target language or skill,
- model useful language,
- guide the student through noticing and controlled or semi-controlled use,
- prepare for freer use later.

Recommended internal flow:

- notice / model / identify,
- guided use / short response / correction.

Good 1:1 online activity types:

- short dialogue analysis,
- sentence matching,
- error correction,
- reorder the conversation,
- choose the better sentence,
- complete the response,
- compare two model answers,
- read and highlight target language,
- substitution drill with communicative meaning,
- guided transformation.

### Activate / Freer Use + Wrap-Up

Purpose:

- let the student use the lesson language more independently,
- move from accuracy toward fluency or personal expression,
- end with evidence that the student can actually do the lesson goal.

Good 1:1 online activity types:

- mini role-play,
- one-minute talk,
- question-and-answer exchange,
- compare-and-choose task,
- short problem-solving task,
- teacher interview,
- describe-and-explain task,
- short message or paragraph creation,
- read-and-respond task,
- opinion + reason challenge.

## The key rule: same spine, rotating activity families

To prevent boredom, Sentivo should not repeat the same task formats lesson after lesson. Instead, writers should rotate **activity families** inside the fixed lesson spine.[web:1834][web:1838]

### Suggested activity-family rotation for one-to-one online classes

| Stage | Activity family options |
|---|---|
| Engage | preference question, picture prompt, prediction, either/or, memory check, mini poll |
| Study | highlight-and-find, compare-two-models, reorder, error-correct, complete-the-dialogue, reformulate |
| Activate | role-play, one-minute talk, teacher interview, opinion challenge, short writing + read aloud, problem-solving |

## What should stay the same in every lesson?

- the 3-stage timing structure,
- the presence of a visible lesson goal or can-do,
- a movement from easier to freer use,
- at least one moment of actual student production,
- a fast recap at the end.[web:1623][web:1820]

## What should change from lesson to lesson?

- the exact warm-up type,
- the kind of model-analysis task,
- the form of guided practice,
- the freer task format,
- the interaction style within 1:1 constraints,
- the medium used (voice, chat, image, short text, prompt card).

## Recommended design rule for writers

For each lesson, require:

- one Engage task,
- two short Study chunks,
- one Activate task,
- one recap or exit check,
- a different activity-family combination from the previous lesson whenever possible.

# 9-Slide Lesson Pattern Framework for 25-Minute One-to-One Online Classes

This framework turns Sentivo's lesson structure into a **repeatable slide system** that stays consistent for teachers while still feeling varied for students. It is designed specifically for **online one-to-one classes**, **25 minutes per lesson**, and **9 slides per lesson**.[web:1826][web:1835][web:1820]

The main idea is:

- every lesson has the **same 9-slide backbone**,
- the **activity type inside the backbone rotates**,
- early units use simpler single-activity highlights,
- middle units combine two activities,
- late units also combine activities but with slightly higher difficulty and less support, so students can feel progress inside the same CEFR level.[web:1800][web:1804][web:1619]

## Core 9-slide lesson structure

Every lesson should contain these nine slides in this order.

| Slide | Function | Purpose |
|---|---|---|
| Title | Lesson opening | Introduce the lesson topic clearly and simply |
| Warm-up | Personal activation | Get the learner speaking quickly |
| Vocabulary | Target language set-up | Pre-teach or recycle useful words |
| Highlight | Main lesson focus | Present the key grammar, phrase set, reading focus, or communicative aim |
| Practice | Guided use | Help the learner notice and use the target |
| Activity | Main performance task | Use the lesson focus in a communicative task |
| Reading or Speaking | Skill expansion | Add a second skill focus or a short extension |
| Review | Recall and correction | Recycle the target and fix errors |
| Wrap-up | Can-do close | Confirm achievement and end cleanly |

## Stable structure, variable activity design

The lesson structure should stay the same, but the **activity family** inside each slide should rotate so the experience does not become repetitive.[web:1836][web:1839]

Examples:

- Warm-up can be a question, a picture prompt, a choice task, or a memory challenge.
- Vocabulary can be matching, image-labeling, category sort, or teacher-led elicitation.
- Highlight can be a dialogue, a mini reading, a sentence pattern board, an error-noticing task, or a comparison of two model answers.
- Activity can be a role-play, one-minute talk, interview, information gap, message writing, or compare-and-choose challenge.

## Recommended 9-slide pattern details

### Title

Purpose:

- establish the topic,
- make the lesson feel clear and focused,
- orient the student instantly.

### Warm-up

Purpose:

- get the learner talking within the first minute,
- connect to the lesson topic,
- reduce passivity in online one-to-one classes.[web:1826]

Good warm-up types:

- quick personal question,
- either-or choice,
- picture reaction,
- prediction,
- mini poll,
- “tell me one thing about...” prompt.

### Vocabulary

Purpose:

- set up the language needed for the lesson,
- reduce friction later in speaking or reading,
- make the learner feel prepared.

Good tasks:

- match word to picture,
- choose the correct word,
- teacher describes and student guesses,
- sort words into categories,
- quick pronunciation repeat and personalize.

### Highlight

Purpose:

- present the core focus of the lesson,
- show the main sentence pattern, mini text, model dialogue, or target skill.

The highlight does **not** have to be grammar only. It can be:

- a model dialogue,
- a useful phrase set,
- a mini reading,
- a speaking structure,
- a comparison between weak and strong answers,
- a short functional language set.

### Practice

Purpose:

- move from noticing to guided control,
- help the learner use the target safely before freer work.

### Activity

Purpose:

- create the main communicative moment of the lesson,
- make the student actually use the target language.[web:1823][web:1827]

Good tasks:

- role-play,
- one-minute talk,
- teacher interview,
- compare-and-choose,
- simple problem-solving,
- message writing then reading aloud,
- mini storytelling,
- opinion plus reasons,
- describe and recommend.

### Reading or Speaking

Purpose:

- add one more skill layer,
- reinforce the lesson focus through a second mode,
- avoid making the lesson feel one-dimensional.

### Review

Purpose:

- recycle vocabulary and target language,
- check what the student can now do,
- fix small errors before the lesson ends.

### Wrap-up

Purpose:

- end clearly,
- restate the achievement,
- make the student feel progress.

## Unit band progression across the 12 units

The same 9-slide structure should be used across the whole level, but the **inner difficulty and activity design** should shift by band.[web:1800][web:1716]

### Foundation band

This band covers the first four units.

Design principle:

- one clear focus,
- one main activity,
- strong scaffolding,
- simple slide tasks,
- limited language combination.

### Combination band

This band covers the next four units.

Design principle:

- mix or combine two activities,
- ask the learner to connect two language moves,
- increase text or response length slightly,
- reduce scaffolding a little.

### Bridge band

This band covers the final four units.

Design principle:

- combine activities more naturally,
- make speaking or writing slightly more independent,
- increase connectedness,
- show visible progress toward the next level without actually crossing into it.[web:1803][web:1619]

## How to keep lessons from feeling boring

The structure should stay stable, but the **activity families** must rotate.[web:1839][web:1838]

### Recommended warm-up rotation

## Topic design guidance

Topics should stay relevant to English learning and to practical communication. They should support vocabulary growth, sentence building, reading, speaking, and functional communication.

Good topic families include:

- daily routines
- hobbies and interests
- school life
- work and jobs
- food and eating out
- shopping
- travel and transport
- family and relationships
- health and habits
- entertainment and media
- places in town
- nature and weather
- technology in daily life
- celebrations and traditions
- study skills
- communication and feelings

## Sample topic feel by band

### Foundation band topic feel

Topics should be concrete and everyday:

- at school
- after class
- my room
- favorite food
- getting around
- hobbies at home
- weekend plans
- shopping for clothes

### Combination band topic feel

Topics can widen and include simple comparison or choice:

- healthy habits
- online shopping
- city life and country life
- travel with friends
- choosing a good gift
- clubs and free-time activities
- movies and recommendations
- daily routines in different places

### Bridge band topic feel

Topics can become slightly more reflective or opinion-based while staying in level:

- balancing study and free time
- the best place to relax
- habits I want to change
- useful technology in daily life
- good ways to learn English
- planning a better weekend
- what makes a good friend
- activities that help you feel better

## Final recommendation

For Sentivo’s 25-minute one-to-one online curriculum:

- use **one stable 9-slide lesson framework**,
- let early units share the same broad pattern,
- let middle-band units combine two activities more often,
- let late-band units combine tasks with slightly higher independence and complexity,
- rotate activity families so lessons feel fresh,
- use relevant English-learning topics that are practical and communicative.
