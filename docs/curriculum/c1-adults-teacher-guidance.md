# Adults C1 — Teacher Guidance

Full per-lesson teacher guidance for all six Adults C1 units, per the
C1/C2 framework's requirement that guidance be "much more detailed than at
lower levels." Format confirmed against Unit 1 as a pilot before the
remaining five units were produced at the same depth.

Each lesson includes: purpose, what to elicit, what to listen/look for,
likely strong-B2 responses (the actual ceiling this teaching targets),
concrete upgrade paths, exact intervention language, which Scorecard
dimensions apply, an extension, and a recovery option.

**Scorecard dimension emphasis by mode** (used throughout, not just here):
Performance lessons prioritize **Naturalness, Interaction Control,
Pragmatics** (live, real-time control under pressure); Model lessons
prioritize **Precision, Discourse Control, Advanced Range** (noticing how
strong language is built); Output lessons prioritize **Precision, Discourse
Control, Register Control** (constructing controlled written language).
Register Control and Pragmatics can apply anywhere register or face-saving
is live, regardless of mode — treat the above as defaults, not walls.

# Unit 1: Argument and Disagreement — Teacher Guidance

### Mode order: Performance → Model → Output

## Lesson 1 (Performance) — Timed Opinion Turn + light Rapid Reformulation

**Purpose:** Get learners producing fast, structured opinions under mild
time pressure, and immediately reformulating for clearer stance-taking —
the entry skill the rest of the unit builds on.

**Elicit:** 60–90 second opinion turns on controversial-but-low-stakes
prompts (e.g. "Should companies ban smartphones in meetings?" / "Is remote
work better than office work?"). One prep-free attempt, then one immediate
reformulation of the same turn.

**Listen for:** Whether the stance is clear in the first 10 seconds; whether
reasons are distinct (not restatements of the stance); whether hedging is
present but not overused; filler/false-start density under time pressure.

**Likely strong-B2 responses:**
- "I think remote work is better because you can save time and also it's
  more comfortable, and, um, also flexible."
- "In my opinion, banning phones is a good idea because people don't pay
  attention if they have their phone."

**Upgrade path:**
- Flat stance → sharpened stance: "I think X is better" → "I'd argue X has
  a clear edge over Y, at least for most people."
- Restated reasons → distinct reasons: replace a second reason that just
  re-says the first with a genuinely different angle (cost vs. wellbeing vs.
  output quality).
- Filler-heavy hedge → controlled hedge: "um, also" → "and, more
  importantly,"

**Intervention prompts (say these live):**
- "Give me your stance in one sentence — no reasons yet."
- "That second reason sounds like the first one again — give me a
  different angle."
- "Say it again, but cut every 'um' and replace it with a half-second
  pause."

**Scorecard focus:** Naturalness (chunking, disfluency control), Pragmatics
(hedging that reads as considered, not uncertain).

**Extension:** Ask for a third, less obvious reason, or a one-sentence
counter-argument they'd concede exists.

**Recovery:** If the student stalls, give a sentence frame: "I'd say ___,
mainly because ___ — though I get that ___." Let them fill it once before
asking for a frame-free retry.

## Lesson 2 (Performance) — Challenge & Pushback

**Purpose:** Practice holding, softening, or refining a position in real
time when directly challenged — the core C1 skill of staying composed and
precise under interpersonal pressure.

**Elicit:** Learner states a position from Lesson 1's topic pool; teacher
or partner pushes back with a pointed counter-question or objection;
learner must respond within a few seconds.

**Listen for:** Does the learner concede too fast (abandons position) or
too slow (repeats the same point louder)? Do they acknowledge the
challenge before responding? Is the pushback response substantive or just
a hedge?

**Likely strong-B2 responses:**
- "No, I don't agree, I still think remote work is better."
- "Yes but, um, not everyone has a good place to work at home."

**Upgrade path:**
- Flat disagreement → acknowledged disagreement: "No, I don't agree" →
  "That's fair, but I'd still say..."
- Bare concession → conditional concession: "Not everyone has a good place"
  → "You're right that it depends on the setup — for people without a
  proper workspace, the case weakens."

**Intervention prompts:**
- "Don't say 'no' first — acknowledge what's true in their point, then
  push back."
- "Give me a condition under which you'd actually change your mind."
- "That was a full retreat — find one part of your original point you can
  still defend."

**Scorecard focus:** Pragmatics (face-saving, tact), Interaction Control
(recovering/holding a turn under challenge).

**Extension:** Partner issues a second, sharper challenge that specifically
targets the concession just made.

**Recovery:** If the student goes silent, supply a starter: "That's a fair
point, but..." and let them complete it before retrying unaided.

## Lesson 3 (Performance) — Negotiated Decision (short scenario)

**Purpose:** Move from defending one's own opinion to reaching a joint
decision when priorities conflict — introduces compromise language ahead
of Unit 5's full negotiation focus.

**Elicit:** Pairs choose between two options with real trade-offs (e.g.
two vendors, two policies) where each partner is given a different
priority card; they must agree on one option in under 3 minutes.

**Listen for:** Whether learners propose (not just object), whether they
name the trade-off explicitly, whether the final agreement is stated
clearly by both sides.

**Likely strong-B2 responses:**
- "I think we should choose A because it's cheaper."
- "Okay, maybe B is also fine."

**Upgrade path:**
- Bare proposal → justified proposal with a concession offered up front:
  "I think we should choose A" → "I'd lean toward A for the cost — I know
  that means slower delivery, which I can live with if you can."
- Vague agreement → explicit agreement: "maybe B is also fine" → "Let's go
  with B, on the condition that we revisit cost in a month."

**Intervention prompts:**
- "Name the trade-off out loud before you propose."
- "Don't just agree — restate what you're each giving up."
- "Close it: say the final decision in one sentence you both confirm."

**Scorecard focus:** Discourse Control (proposing/conceding structure),
Interaction Control (closing a negotiation cleanly).

**Extension:** Add a third, higher-stakes priority card mid-negotiation and
have them re-open the agreement.

**Recovery:** If neither proposes, prompt one side directly: "You go
first — what's your opening offer?"

## Lesson 4 (Model) — Timed Opinion Turn, light framing

**Purpose:** Shift from producing to noticing — analyze how a strong
opinion piece or short talk opens, structures, and closes an argument,
then reuse those moves in shorter framed turns.

**Elicit:** Learners read/watch a short strong opinion model (structured:
stance → 2 reasons → acknowledgment of counterpoint → restated stance),
then annotate its structure before giving their own framed 30-second
version on a new topic.

**Listen for/Look for:** Can learners name what the model does at each
stage (not just "it's a good argument")? Do their own framed turns borrow
the structural moves (not just the topic)?

**Likely strong-B2 responses (in noticing):** "The speaker gives their
opinion and then says two reasons." (accurate but structurally shallow —
misses the counterpoint-acknowledgment move entirely.)

**Upgrade path:** Push noticing past "reasons" toward discourse moves:
where does the speaker signal a shift to the counterpoint ("That said,"
"To be fair,")? How do they close without just repeating the opening
line?

**Intervention prompts:**
- "Find the sentence where the speaker admits the other side has a point.
  What word signals that shift?"
- "The ending isn't just a repeat — what's different about how it's said
  the second time?"
- "Now do the same shape, your topic, 30 seconds."

**Scorecard focus:** Discourse Control (signposting, concession
placement), Advanced Range (noticing, not yet producing, contrastive
framing).

**Extension:** Compare two models with different structures (one
concedes early, one concedes late) and ask which is more persuasive and
why.

**Recovery:** If annotation stalls, give a partially-filled structure map
(stance / reason 1 / reason 2 / ___ / close) and have them find just the
missing piece.

## Lesson 5 (Model) — Mediation Brief, guided (2 short sources)

**Purpose:** Practice fair, accurate summarizing of two opposing
viewpoints before showing where they diverge — the core move of
mediation, introduced here through guided analysis rather than free
production.

**Elicit:** Two short contrasting texts/clips on one issue; learners
produce a spoken summary that represents both fairly, then explicitly
states the point of disagreement.

**Listen for:** Loaded language that reveals the learner's own bias while
"summarizing"; whether the disagreement point is precisely located (not
just "they disagree").

**Likely strong-B2 responses:** "One person thinks it's good and the
other person thinks it's bad." (technically neutral, but locates the
disagreement so vaguely it's not useful to a real reader.)

**Upgrade path:** Vague opposition → precisely located disagreement: "one
thinks good, one thinks bad" → "they actually agree the policy helps
efficiency — the real disagreement is over who bears the cost."

**Intervention prompts:**
- "Where exactly do they stop agreeing? Point to the sentence."
- "You used 'obviously' just now — that's your opinion leaking in. Say it
  neutrally."
- "Summarize side A in one sentence with no adjectives."

**Scorecard focus:** Precision (locating the exact point of divergence),
Pragmatics (neutral framing, avoiding leaked stance).

**Extension:** Add a third source that partially agrees with each side and
ask the learner to place it accurately.

**Recovery:** If bias keeps leaking, have the learner summarize the side
they personally disagree with first, out loud, before touching the other.

## Lesson 6 (Model) — Negotiated Decision, guided model analysis

**Purpose:** Map how a model speaker proposes, concedes, and reframes
during a decision dialogue, building the pattern vocabulary Lesson 3
used more instinctively.

**Elicit:** Learners listen to/read a model negotiation dialogue and label
each turn (propose / concede / reframe / close), then explain in their
own words what makes one concession sound genuine vs. grudging.

**Listen for/Look for:** Whether learners can distinguish a real
concession from a token one; whether they notice reframing (restating the
other's point in more workable terms) as distinct from just agreeing.

**Likely strong-B2 responses:** "This person agrees with that person."
(collapses concession and reframe into one undifferentiated "agreement.")

**Upgrade path:** Push toward the distinction: agreeing = accepting the
point as-is; reframing = restating it in a way that opens a new option.
Have them find one reframe and explain what changed.

**Intervention prompts:**
- "Is that a real concession or just a filler agreement? What's the
  difference?"
- "Find where someone takes the other person's complaint and turns it
  into a new option — that's reframing, not agreeing."
- "Label all four moves in this exchange."

**Scorecard focus:** Discourse Control (labeling functional moves),
Advanced Range (reframing language, conditional structures).

**Extension:** Have learners rewrite one grudging concession in the model
to sound genuine, and one genuine one to sound grudging — compare what
changed linguistically.

**Recovery:** If labeling is inconsistent, restrict to just propose vs.
concede first, add reframe once that distinction is solid.

## Lesson 7 (Output) — Mediation Brief → written mediation/recommendation note

**Purpose:** Transfer the Lesson 5 mediation skill into writing — a short
note that fairly frames a dispute and recommends a balanced course of
action for a reader who wasn't present.

**Elicit:** A written mediation note (roughly 120–160 words) on a dispute
from the unit's topic pool, addressed to a named third-party
decision-maker.

**Listen for/Look for:** Whether both sides get proportionate space (not
one paragraph vs. one sentence); whether the recommendation follows
logically from the stated trade-offs rather than appearing out of
nowhere; register appropriate to a workplace note, not a casual opinion.

**Likely strong-B2 responses:** A note that summarizes both sides
adequately but ends with "So I think we should do A" with no bridge from
the trade-offs just described.

**Upgrade path:** Unearned conclusion → earned conclusion: insert a
transition that explicitly weighs the trade-offs before the
recommendation ("Given that cost matters more here than speed, ___").
Casual closer ("So I think...") → professional closer ("On balance, I'd
recommend...").

**Intervention prompts (as written feedback, delivered live in
class):**
- "Your recommendation appears with no bridge — add one sentence that
  weighs what you just described."
- "'So I think' is too casual for this reader — try 'On balance' or
  'Given the above.'"
- "Side B gets one sentence and side A gets four — even it out."

**Scorecard focus:** Discourse Control (earned conclusions, proportion),
Register Control (professional written tone).

**Extension:** Rewrite the same note for a more senior/skeptical reader
who will push back on the recommendation.

**Recovery:** If the student can't bridge trade-off to recommendation,
supply the connector phrase bank ("Given that... / On balance... /
Weighing both...") and have them pick and complete one.

## Lesson 8 (Output) — Negotiated Decision → written recommendation/decision summary

**Purpose:** Write up the outcome of a negotiation (like Lesson 3's) for
stakeholders who weren't in the room — requires stating not just the
decision but the reasoning and trade-offs accepted.

**Elicit:** A short written decision summary (roughly 100–140 words)
covering: the decision, the reasoning, and one trade-off knowingly
accepted.

**Listen for/Look for:** Whether the trade-off is stated as a trade-off
(acknowledged cost) rather than omitted or buried; whether the summary
reads as a record for someone who wasn't there, not a diary of the
conversation.

**Likely strong-B2 responses:** "We decided to choose A because it was
cheaper." (true but omits the accepted trade-off entirely, reading as
incomplete to an absent stakeholder.)

**Upgrade path:** Omitted trade-off → stated trade-off: add "...though
this means slower delivery, which we agreed was an acceptable cost given
the budget." Conversational recount ("we talked about...") → decision
record register ("The team selected... on the basis that...").

**Intervention prompts:**
- "A stakeholder reading this needs to know what you gave up — where's
  that sentence?"
- "'We talked about' is a narration of the meeting — this needs to read
  like a record, not a diary."
- "Cut this by a third — decision summaries are read by busy people."

**Scorecard focus:** Precision (stating trade-offs exactly), Register
Control (decision-record tone vs. conversational recount).

**Extension:** Add a one-sentence contingency ("if X happens, we'll
revisit") to the summary.

**Recovery:** If the trade-off is missing, ask directly: "What did the
losing option have that this one doesn't?" and have them write that
sentence first.

## Lesson 9 (Output) — Timed Opinion Turn → structured opinion/reflection piece

**Purpose:** Close the unit by converting a spoken stance (Lesson 1-style)
into a structured written opinion or reflection piece — the unit's full
speaking-to-writing arc completes here.

**Elicit:** A written opinion/reflection piece (roughly 150–180 words) on
a recurring disagreement (not a one-off dispute), following stance → two
distinct reasons → acknowledged counterpoint → restated stance — the same
shape noticed in Lesson 4's model.

**Listen for/Look for:** Whether the structure noticed in Lesson 4
actually shows up in the learner's own writing; whether the restated
stance at the end adds something (nuance, condition) rather than just
repeating the opening sentence verbatim.

**Likely strong-B2 responses:** A piece with a clear stance and two
reasons but no counterpoint acknowledgment, and a closing sentence
identical to the opening.

**Upgrade path:** Missing counterpoint → inserted concession paragraph
using a Lesson 4-style signal ("That said, / To be fair,"). Repeated
closer → earned closer: restate the stance with an added condition or
consequence ("...which is why, unless X changes, I'd still choose Y.")

**Intervention prompts:**
- "Where's the sentence that admits the other side has a point? This
  piece doesn't have one yet."
- "Your last sentence is your first sentence again — what can you add
  this time that you couldn't say at the start?"
- "Read Lesson 4's model closing again — what's different about how it
  restates the stance?"

**Scorecard focus:** Discourse Control (full argument shape, earned
closing), Advanced Range (concession framing, conditional restatement).

**Extension:** Have the learner write a one-paragraph rebuttal to their
own piece, then a one-sentence response to that rebuttal.

**Recovery:** If the counterpoint paragraph is missing under time
pressure, allow it as a single added sentence rather than a full
paragraph — partial credit for the move, refine length next time.

# Unit 2: Mediation and Synthesis — Teacher Guidance

### Mode order: Model → Output → Performance

## Lesson 1 (Model) — Timed Opinion Turn, light framing around a "mediator's voice" sample

**Purpose:** Introduce the mediator's stance — neutral, balance-seeking —
by contrast with Unit 1's advocacy stance, before learners try to hold it
themselves.

**Elicit:** Learners listen to/read a short model that frames both sides
of an issue without taking one, then give a 30-second opinion turn on a
new topic that deliberately emphasizes balance over advocacy.

**Listen for:** Whether the "balanced" turn secretly still argues for one
side (a common failure mode — learners default to advocacy because that's
what Unit 1 trained); whether both sides get comparable verbal space.

**Likely strong-B2 responses:** "Some people think A, but I think B is
better." (frames it as balance, but the "but I think" gives away the
advocacy underneath.)

**Upgrade path:** Hidden advocacy → genuine balance: replace "but I think
B is better" with "and the disagreement really comes down to..." — locate
the crux instead of picking a side.

**Intervention prompts:**
- "You just picked a side again — where's the sentence that stays
  neutral?"
- "Give both sides the same number of sentences. Count them."
- "Don't end on your opinion — end on where the two views actually
  diverge."

**Scorecard focus:** Pragmatics (suppressing stance leakage), Precision
(accurately representing both positions).

**Extension:** Have the learner mediate a topic where they have a strong
personal opinion, to stress-test neutrality under real pressure.

**Recovery:** If genuine balance feels impossible, let them draft it in
writing first (slower, more controllable) before attempting it live.

---

## Lesson 2 (Model) — Mediation Brief, guided (2 short sources)

**Purpose:** Practice summarizing two texts fairly and then explicitly
linking or contrasting them — the analytic half of mediation, prerequisite
to Lesson 4's written version.

**Elicit:** Two short texts on the same disagreement; a spoken summary of
each, followed by one sentence naming how they relate (agree/partially
overlap/directly conflict).

**Listen for:** Whether the relationship sentence is precise (not just
"they're different") and whether the two summaries are genuinely
independent rather than the second one just negating the first.

**Likely strong-B2 responses:** "Text A says X and text B says the
opposite." (the relationship is real but stated at the lowest possible
resolution — no sense of where or how much they diverge.)

**Upgrade path:** "the opposite" → located divergence: "Text A and B
actually agree on the underlying problem — they only disagree on who
should pay for the fix."

**Intervention prompts:**
- "'The opposite' isn't specific enough — what exactly do they disagree
  on?"
- "Is there anything these two sources actually agree on? Find it."
- "Say the relationship in one sentence without using the word
  'different.'"

**Scorecard focus:** Precision (locating agreement/disagreement exactly),
Discourse Control (structuring a two-source comparison).

**Extension:** Add a third source that agrees with neither fully and ask
where it fits.

**Recovery:** Provide a simple relationship-type menu (agree / partially
overlap / flatly conflict / talk past each other) and have the learner
pick one before justifying it.

---

## Lesson 3 (Model) — Negotiated Decision, guided model analysis

**Purpose:** Map how a model mediator summarizes, checks understanding,
and proposes options — the exact three moves Lesson 9 (Performance) will
require live.

**Elicit:** A model mediation dialogue; learners label each mediator turn
as summarize / check / propose, then explain what would go wrong if the
mediator skipped the "check" step.

**Listen for/Look for:** Whether learners can articulate *why* checking
matters (not just spot it) — the risk of summarizing inaccurately and
having neither party correct it.

**Likely strong-B2 responses:** "The mediator asks if that's right."
(spots the check move but doesn't connect it to a real risk being
managed.)

**Upgrade path:** Push from spotting to functional understanding: "What
happens to the rest of the conversation if the mediator's summary was
wrong and nobody caught it?"

**Intervention prompts:**
- "Label this turn: is the mediator summarizing, checking, or proposing?"
- "Find the exact phrase the mediator uses to check understanding — what
  would you say instead?"
- "What's the risk of skipping the check?"

**Scorecard focus:** Discourse Control (functional move labeling),
Interaction Control (checking as a turn-management strategy).

**Extension:** Have learners rewrite the dialogue with the check step
removed and predict where it breaks down.

**Recovery:** If labeling all three moves is too much at once, isolate
"check" alone first — have them find every checking phrase in the
transcript before attempting the other two labels.

---

## Lesson 4 (Output) — Mediation Brief → written mediation/recommendation note

**Purpose:** Write a mediation brief for a decision-maker that explains
both sides and recommends a next step — building directly on Lessons 1–3's
neutrality and summarizing skills, now in writing with careful
attribution.

**Elicit:** A written brief (120–160 words) that names both parties'
positions accurately, attributes claims to the right side, and closes with
a neutral-toned recommendation.

**Listen for/Look for:** Misattribution (crediting a claim to the wrong
side); a recommendation that suddenly breaks neutral tone after two
paragraphs of careful balance.

**Likely strong-B2 responses:** A fairly balanced two-paragraph summary
that ends with "I think we should just go with what Team A wants" — tone
snaps from neutral-analytic to personal-casual in the last line.

**Upgrade path:** Tone snap → sustained register: replace "I think we
should just go with..." with "On balance, Team A's position better
addresses the immediate risk, though Team B's cost concern should still be
tracked."

**Intervention prompts:**
- "Check every sentence — whose claim is this, and did you say so?"
- "Your recommendation sentence sounds like a different person wrote it —
  match the tone of the paragraph before it."
- "Acknowledge the losing side's point even while recommending against
  it."

**Scorecard focus:** Precision (attribution accuracy), Register Control
(sustained neutral-analytic tone through to the recommendation).

**Extension:** Have the learner write the same brief for an audience that
already favors one side, keeping the analysis identical but adjusting how
the recommendation is framed.

**Recovery:** If attribution keeps slipping, have the learner literally
label each sentence "[A]" or "[B]" in a draft before smoothing it into
prose.

## Lesson 5 (Output) — Negotiated Decision → written decision summary for absent stakeholders

**Purpose:** Record a compromise, its reasoning, and any conditions for
readers who weren't in the room — extends Lesson 4's neutral register into
recording an already-reached agreement rather than recommending one.

**Elicit:** A written decision summary (100–140 words) covering: what was
agreed, why, and any conditions attached to the agreement.

**Listen for/Look for:** Whether conditions are stated as binding
("provided that...") rather than vague ("hopefully..."); whether the
reasoning is present at all, or just the bare outcome.

**Likely strong-B2 responses:** "Team A and Team B agreed to do X."
(states the outcome but gives an absent reader nothing to evaluate it
with — no reasoning, no conditions.)

**Upgrade path:** Bare outcome → reasoned record: add "...on the grounds
that it addressed both the timeline risk and the budget concern, provided
that costs are reviewed again in Q2."

**Intervention prompts:**
- "An absent reader needs to know *why* — where's that sentence?"
- "Is this agreement conditional on anything? Say the condition
  explicitly, not just implied."
- "Cut anything that isn't outcome, reasoning, or condition."

**Scorecard focus:** Precision (explicit conditions), Discourse Control
(outcome–reasoning–condition structure).

**Extension:** Add a short "what happens if the condition isn't met"
clause.

**Recovery:** Supply the three-part frame (Decision: ___ / Reasoning:
___ / Condition: ___) for a first draft, then have them rewrite it as
connected prose.

## Lesson 6 (Output) — Timed Opinion Turn → structured stance/reflection piece on recurring conflict patterns

**Purpose:** Synthesize across multiple mediation cases into a reflective
piece about how to handle a *type* of conflict, not just one instance —
the unit's shift from case-mediation to pattern-recognition.

**Elicit:** A written reflective/advisory piece (roughly 150 words) on how
to handle a recurring category of conflict (e.g. "when priorities clash
between departments"), drawing on patterns noticed across the unit so far.

**Listen for/Look for:** Whether the piece stays at the level of one
example (a case study) or actually generalizes to a pattern; whether any
general claim is still backed by a concrete illustration.

**Likely strong-B2 responses:** A piece that retells one specific
conflict in detail without ever stepping back to say what it illustrates
more broadly.

**Upgrade path:** Single-case retelling → generalized pattern with
example: add a framing sentence ("This kind of conflict usually comes down
to unclear ownership, not bad intent") before or after the illustrative
case.

**Intervention prompts:**
- "This is one story — what's the general pattern it's an example of?"
- "State the pattern first, in one sentence, then use your example to
  support it."
- "Don't lose the concrete detail entirely — a pure abstraction is just as
  weak as a pure anecdote."

**Scorecard focus:** Advanced Range (generalization/exemplification
structures — "tends to," "more often than not"), Discourse Control
(pattern-then-example architecture).

**Extension:** Ask for a second, different example of the same pattern to
confirm it generalizes.

**Recovery:** If generalizing feels too abstract, ask directly: "If this
happened again with different people, what would probably still be true?"
— their answer usually is the pattern sentence.

## Lesson 7 (Performance) — Timed Opinion Turn + light Rapid Reformulation

**Purpose:** Return neutrality to live, unscripted speech under time
pressure — harder than the guided Model/Output work because there's no
text to lean on.

**Elicit:** Quick personal takes on complex multi-stakeholder situations
(e.g. "Who should decide school reopening policy — parents, teachers, or
government?"), then immediate reformulation for more neutral framing.

**Listen for:** Whether the live, unprepared version is noticeably less
neutral than the written work from Lessons 4–6 (expected — this is the
point of the lesson, not a failure).

**Likely strong-B2 responses:** "Honestly I think parents should decide,
because teachers don't know each family." (a real, unhedged opinion —
appropriate raw material, not yet the target output.)

**Upgrade path:** Raw opinion → framed-as-complexity: "There's a real
tension here between local family knowledge and consistent public-health
judgment — I lean toward parents having more say, but I get why that
worries public-health people."

**Intervention prompts:**
- "That was a straight opinion — now reframe it as: 'there's a tension
  between X and Y.'"
- "Say what worries the side you disagree with, in their own terms."
- "Keep your lean, but surround it with the complexity you just skipped."

**Scorecard focus:** Naturalness (neutral framing that doesn't sound
stilted), Pragmatics (acknowledging opposing concerns credibly).

**Extension:** Push to a second, more polarizing topic to see if the
framing move holds up under stronger personal pull.

**Recovery:** If the reformulation just repeats the original opinion with
softer words, supply the frame "There's a real tension between ___ and
___" as a sentence starter.

## Lesson 8 (Performance) — Challenge & Pushback

**Purpose:** Hold a mediating position — not an advocacy position — while
being challenged from both sides at once, the hardest interaction-control
task in the unit.

**Elicit:** Learner proposes how to handle a conflict; two partners (or
teacher, alternating) challenge from each side's perspective in quick
succession.

**Listen for:** Whether the learner drifts into defending one side once
challenged (common under pressure); whether they can absorb challenge from
both directions without contradicting their own prior statement.

**Likely strong-B2 responses:** After being pushed by side A: "Okay,
maybe you're right, we should prioritize that instead." (fully caves to
whichever side just spoke — loses the mediating position entirely.)

**Upgrade path:** Full cave → held position with acknowledgment: "That's a
fair concern, and I'd factor it in, but I don't think it changes the
overall balance I proposed."

**Intervention prompts:**
- "You just switched sides — what happened to your original proposal?"
- "Acknowledge the concern without abandoning your position. Try again."
- "Say what would actually need to change for you to revise your
  proposal."

**Scorecard focus:** Interaction Control (holding a position under
bidirectional pressure), Pragmatics (face-saving for both challengers).

**Extension:** Have both challengers push simultaneously (overlapping
turns) to raise the difficulty further.

**Recovery:** If the learner freezes, let them use a stalling-but-controlled
phrase ("Let me take those one at a time...") to buy processing time
without losing the floor.

---

## Lesson 9 (Performance) — Negotiated Decision (short scenario)

**Purpose:** Close the unit by articulating a middle ground live and under
time pressure — the full synthesis of everything mediated in Lessons 1–8.

**Elicit:** A short, high-pressure scenario requiring the learner to
propose a middle-ground resolution within a tight time limit (60–90
seconds), addressed to both parties at once.

**Listen for:** Whether the middle ground is a real synthesis (takes
something from both sides) or just a vague non-answer ("let's all just
compromise somehow").

**Likely strong-B2 responses:** "I think you should both just meet in the
middle." (names the concept of compromise without specifying any actual
content.)

**Upgrade path:** Empty compromise → specified middle ground: "Team A
keeps the earlier deadline, Team B gets the extra reviewer they asked
for — that addresses the main concern on both sides."

**Intervention prompts:**
- "'Meet in the middle' isn't a proposal — what does that actually look
  like?"
- "Name one thing each side gets to keep."
- "Say it as a single sentence both sides could sign off on."

**Scorecard focus:** Discourse Control (concrete proposal structure),
Interaction Control (closing decisively under time pressure).

**Extension:** Introduce a late complication (one side reveals a new
constraint) and require the proposal to be revised on the spot.

**Recovery:** If the proposal stays vague, ask directly: "What's one
concrete thing you'd give Team A, and one concrete thing you'd give Team
B?" and build the sentence from those two answers.

# Unit 3: Professional and Formal Writing — Teacher Guidance

### Mode order: Output → Performance → Model

## Lesson 1 (Output) — Mediation Brief → written synthesis/recommendation note

**Purpose:** Open the unit's writing-first arc: produce a short synthesis
note that integrates multiple sources/viewpoints accurately, before any
spoken rehearsal.

**Elicit:** A written synthesis/recommendation note (120–160 words)
integrating at least two viewpoints or sources on a practical issue.

**Listen for/Look for:** Whether integration is genuine (sources are put
in conversation with each other) or just sequential ("Source A says...
Source B says...") with no synthesis sentence connecting them.

**Likely strong-B2 responses:** Two well-summarized but entirely separate
paragraphs with no connecting sentence at all.

**Upgrade path:** Sequential summary → true synthesis: add a linking
sentence that states what the two sources jointly imply, not just what
each says alone.

**Intervention prompts:**
- "These two paragraphs never talk to each other — add a sentence that
  connects them."
- "What do both sources agree matters, even if they disagree on the
  answer?"
- "If a reader only had time for one sentence, what would it be?"

**Scorecard focus:** Discourse Control (synthesis, not sequencing),
Precision (accurate representation of each source).

**Extension:** Add a third source that complicates the synthesis and
require it to be integrated, not just appended.

**Recovery:** Provide a sentence frame: "Taken together, these suggest
___" and have the learner complete it before revising the rest around it.

---

## Lesson 2 (Output) — Negotiated Decision → written options & recommendation note

**Purpose:** Practice the classic executive-writing move: lay out 2–3
real options, evaluate briefly, recommend one — the backbone shape of
Lesson 7's model analysis.

**Elicit:** A compressed options note (120–150 words): 2–3 options, one
line of evaluation each, one clear recommendation.

**Listen for/Look for:** Whether evaluation is comparative (this option
vs. that one, on a named criterion) or just descriptive (restating what
each option is).

**Likely strong-B2 responses:** "Option A costs less. Option B is faster.
I recommend Option A." (descriptive, not comparative — doesn't say why
cost should win over speed here.)

**Upgrade path:** Descriptive listing → comparative evaluation with a
stated criterion: "Given that budget is the binding constraint this
quarter, Option A's lower cost outweighs Option B's speed advantage."

**Intervention prompts:**
- "You've described the options — now compare them on one criterion that
  actually matters here."
- "Why does that criterion win over the others? Say it."
- "Cut anything that isn't option, evaluation, or recommendation."

**Scorecard focus:** Precision (naming the deciding criterion), Discourse
Control (evaluation before recommendation, not after).

**Extension:** Add a constraint that flips the recommendation (e.g. "the
deadline just moved up") and have them rewrite the conclusion only.

**Recovery:** Supply the criterion explicitly ("cost is the priority
here") if the learner can't identify one, then have them build the
comparison around it.

---

## Lesson 3 (Output) — Timed Opinion Turn → concise position paragraph/email

**Purpose:** Compress a spoken stance into a single paragraph or email
with no padding — the unit's core "economy of language" skill.

**Elicit:** A single paragraph or short email (60–90 words) capturing
position, key reasons, and implied next step, drafted from a prior spoken
stance.

**Listen for/Look for:** Padding — throat-clearing openers ("I wanted to
write to let you know that..."), redundant reason restatement, a next
step that's implied but never actually stated.

**Likely strong-B2 responses:** An email that opens with "I hope this
finds you well, I wanted to reach out regarding..." before getting to any
actual content in sentence three.

**Upgrade path:** Padded opener → direct opener: cut straight to the
position in sentence one; move any pleasantry (if needed at all) to a
single clause, not a sentence.

**Intervention prompts:**
- "Cut your first sentence entirely — does the email still work? It
  usually does."
- "Where's the next step? It's implied, but say it."
- "Read this aloud in 15 seconds — if you can't, cut more."

**Scorecard focus:** Register Control (professional brevity vs.
politeness padding), Discourse Control (explicit next step).

**Extension:** Compress the same email to 40 words without losing the
position, reasons, or next step.

**Recovery:** If cutting feels risky, have the learner draft the padded
version first, then physically cross out every sentence that doesn't
contain position, reason, or next step.

---

## Lesson 4 (Performance) — Timed Opinion Turn + light Rapid Reformulation

**Purpose:** Orally defend a written recommendation to a senior audience
who has only skimmed it — requires re-deriving and compressing the
argument live, not just reading it back.

**Elicit:** Learner explains their Lesson 2 recommendation aloud in under
60 seconds to a "skimmed it, cares about risk/cost/optics/timing"
listener, then reformulates to better mirror those priorities.

**Listen for:** Whether the spoken defense just repeats written phrasing
verbatim (a sign it wasn't internalized) or genuinely re-derives the
point for a listening audience; whether it's framed around what the
listener cares about or around what the writer wanted to say.

**Likely strong-B2 responses:** A word-for-word recitation of the written
note's first paragraph, delivered as if reading, with no adaptation to
the "only skimmed it" listener.

**Upgrade path:** Recitation → listener-framed defense: "You'll have seen
the cost numbers — the short version is, this is the lower-risk option
given the timeline we're under."

**Intervention prompts:**
- "You're reciting your note — talk to me like I only have 30 seconds."
- "Lead with what I care about, not what you wrote first."
- "Say the same point in half the words."

**Scorecard focus:** Naturalness (spoken vs. written register shift),
Pragmatics (framing around listener priorities).

**Extension:** Switch the listener's stated priority mid-turn (from cost
to optics) and require an on-the-fly reframe.

**Recovery:** If the learner can't detach from the written text, take the
note away physically and have them explain "from memory, in your own
words" as a first step.

---

## Lesson 5 (Performance) — Challenge & Pushback

**Purpose:** Handle pointed, skeptical questions about a written
recommendation concisely, without retreating into long explanations —
the C1 skill of staying brief under scrutiny.

**Elicit:** Learner defends their note against pointed questions ("Why
didn't you mention X?" / "What evidence underlies this?" / "What happens
if this fails?"), each answer capped at roughly 15 seconds.

**Listen for:** Over-explaining as a nervous response to challenge (a
classic B2-ceiling pattern — more words instead of sharper ones); whether
the answer to "what if it fails" is a real contingency or a vague
reassurance.

**Likely strong-B2 responses:** A 45-second answer to "why didn't you
mention X" that circles the point three times before landing on "...so
that's basically why I didn't include it."

**Upgrade path:** Circling explanation → direct, capped answer: "X was
out of scope for this decision — it matters, but it doesn't change the
recommendation."

**Intervention prompts:**
- "That took 45 seconds — say it again in 15."
- "'What if it fails' needs a real answer — what would you actually do?"
- "You don't need to justify the omission at length — just say why it
  didn't change the call."

**Scorecard focus:** Discourse Control (concision under pressure),
Pragmatics (non-defensive justification).

**Extension:** Stack two challenges back-to-back with no recovery time
between them.

**Recovery:** If answers keep running long, use a visible timer and stop
the learner mid-sentence at 15 seconds, then have them restart with the
core point first.

---

## Lesson 6 (Performance) — Negotiated Decision (short scenario)

**Purpose:** Reconcile conflicting written recommendations from different
stakeholders live, while keeping references to the documents clear and
economical — bridges written and spoken control.

**Elicit:** Two learners with conflicting written recommendations discuss
and work toward a reconciled position, referring back to specific points
in each note.

**Listen for:** Whether references to the written documents are precise
("your note's second point") or vague ("what you said before"); whether
reconciliation actually resolves the conflict or just states both
positions side by side.

**Likely strong-B2 responses:** "I still think my note is right, but
okay, maybe we can do both." (a non-reconciliation dressed as one — no
actual resolution of the conflict.)

**Upgrade path:** Non-reconciliation → real resolution: "Your note's
concern about cost and mine about timeline aren't actually incompatible —
if we phase the rollout, we address both."

**Intervention prompts:**
- "'Maybe we can do both' — what does that actually mean concretely?"
- "Point to the exact sentence in their note you're responding to."
- "Is this resolved, or are you just avoiding the disagreement?"

**Scorecard focus:** Precision (specific document references), Discourse
Control (genuine resolution vs. surface agreement).

**Extension:** Add a third stakeholder's note mid-discussion that
complicates the reconciliation.

**Recovery:** If reconciliation stalls, have each learner state one thing
they'd be willing to give up from their own note before continuing.

---

## Lesson 7 (Model) — Timed Opinion Turn, light framing

**Purpose:** Analyze exemplary executive texts for structure, density,
stance, and — critically — what gets deliberately left out.

**Elicit:** Learners study a strong one-page memo or leadership email,
identify what was omitted despite being relevant, then give a short
spoken reflection on why the omission strengthens the text.

**Listen for/Look for:** Whether learners can name a *specific* omission
(not just "it's short") and explain why leaving it out helps rather than
just noting that it happened.

**Likely strong-B2 responses:** "It doesn't have a lot of details." (true
but doesn't identify what was cut or why cutting it was the right call.)

**Upgrade path:** Vague brevity-noticing → specific omission analysis:
"It never explains the backstory of the decision — because the reader
who matters here just needs the outcome and the ask."

**Intervention prompts:**
- "What's one thing you'd expect in this memo that isn't there?"
- "Why does leaving that out make it stronger, not weaker?"
- "Now give your own 20-second reflection using that same discipline."

**Scorecard focus:** Advanced Range (analyzing omission as a rhetorical
choice), Discourse Control (selection/prioritization).

**Extension:** Have learners find a text that includes the omitted
material and compare which version serves the stated audience better.

**Recovery:** If nothing seems omitted, prompt with a specific candidate:
"Where's the history of how we got here? Why isn't it in this memo?"

---

## Lesson 8 (Model) — Mediation Brief, guided (2–3 exemplary texts)

**Purpose:** Compare strong examples of concise professional writing and
brief a peer on which suits which audience — analytic precursor to
producing audience-calibrated writing.

**Elicit:** Two or three strong short texts on similar issues; a spoken
briefing to a peer on how they differ in density, tone, and
prioritization, and which fits which audience.

**Listen for/Look for:** Whether "which fits which audience" is justified
by a specific textual feature, or just asserted.

**Likely strong-B2 responses:** "This one is more formal, so it's better
for the board." (correct instinct, unsupported — doesn't name what makes
it formal or why formality is what the board needs.)

**Upgrade path:** Unsupported assertion → evidenced claim: "This one uses
passive constructions and avoids first person — that distancing suits a
board audience more than the direct, first-person voice in the other."

**Intervention prompts:**
- "What specific feature makes you say 'more formal'? Point to it."
- "Why does that feature matter for this particular audience?"
- "Brief me on this like I'm choosing which one to send."

**Scorecard focus:** Precision (evidence-backed claims about register),
Discourse Control (structured audience-fit briefing).

**Extension:** Add a text that's well-written but mismatched to any
plausible audience, and ask the learner to diagnose the mismatch.

**Recovery:** Give a feature checklist (person, passive/active, sentence
length, jargon level) to work through systematically if unstructured
comparison stalls.

---

## Lesson 9 (Model) — Negotiated Decision, guided model analysis

**Purpose:** Map how written briefs frame a meeting and how written
follow-up captures the decision — closing the loop on writing's role in a
live decision process.

**Elicit:** A decision process combining a written packet and a
transcript/recording of the meeting it informed; learners trace what from
the packet got picked up, what got ignored, and how the written follow-up
recorded the outcome.

**Listen for/Look for:** Whether learners notice *specific* moments where
the meeting departed from the packet's framing (not just "the meeting
covered similar ground").

**Likely strong-B2 responses:** "The meeting talked about the same
things as the packet." (misses that meetings often diverge from written
framing in informative ways.)

**Upgrade path:** Sameness-noticing → divergence-tracking: "The packet
led with cost, but the meeting actually spent most of its time on the
timeline risk — the follow-up note should reflect that shift, not just
restate the packet."

**Intervention prompts:**
- "Where did the meeting go somewhere the packet didn't predict?"
- "Does the follow-up note capture that shift, or does it just repeat the
  packet?"
- "What should the next packet do differently, given this?"

**Scorecard focus:** Discourse Control (tracking framing across
documents and live discussion), Advanced Range (noticing implicit shifts
in emphasis).

**Extension:** Have learners draft the improved follow-up note themselves.

**Recovery:** If divergence is hard to spot, isolate one agenda item and
compare packet framing to meeting discussion for that item alone.

# Unit 4: Presentation and Briefing — Teacher Guidance

### Mode order: Performance → Model → Output

## Lesson 1 (Performance) — Timed Opinion Turn + light Rapid Reformulation

**Purpose:** Deliver a very short "stand-up" briefing under time pressure
and immediately sharpen its narrative, audience framing, and call to
action — the unit's entry skill.

**Elicit:** A 45–60 second mini-briefing on a high-stakes topic (a change
initiative, an incident, a visible project), then immediate reformulation.

**Listen for:** Whether there's an identifiable call to action at all;
whether the briefing opens with context (slow) or with the headline
(fast).

**Likely strong-B2 responses:** A briefing that spends 30 of its 45
seconds on background before mentioning what's actually needed from the
listener.

**Upgrade path:** Context-first → headline-first: open with the ask or
the key fact, then backfill only the context needed to support it.

**Intervention prompts:**
- "What do you need from me? Say that first, not last."
- "Cut the background to one sentence — what's the minimum I need to
  understand the ask?"
- "Say it again, headline first."

**Scorecard focus:** Discourse Control (headline-first structuring),
Naturalness (briefing register vs. narrative register).

**Extension:** Compress the same briefing to 20 seconds.

**Recovery:** If the ask never surfaces, ask directly: "In one sentence,
what do you want me to do?" and build backward from that answer.

---

## Lesson 2 (Performance) — Challenge & Pushback (Q&A-under-challenge)

**Purpose:** Handle unscripted, skeptical Q&A from powerful stakeholders
after a brief presentation — composure and concision under real social
pressure.

**Elicit:** After a short briefing, learner fields 2–3 unscripted tough
questions from a "senior leadership" role-player.

**Listen for:** Over-explaining as a stress response; whether composure
holds (measured pace, no defensive tone) or cracks (rushed, apologetic).

**Likely strong-B2 responses:** A rushed, run-on answer that tries to
address every possible objection at once rather than the specific
question asked.

**Upgrade path:** Scattershot defense → targeted answer: pause briefly,
answer the specific question asked, stop — resist the urge to
pre-emptively defend against unasked objections.

**Intervention prompts:**
- "Answer only the question I asked — not the three you're afraid I'll
  ask next."
- "Take a breath before answering. You don't need to fill the silence
  immediately."
- "That was defensive in tone — say it again, matter-of-fact."

**Scorecard focus:** Interaction Control (composure, turn discipline),
Pragmatics (non-defensive tone under status pressure).

**Extension:** Have the "senior leadership" role-player interrupt
mid-answer to raise difficulty further.

**Recovery:** If composure breaks down, allow an explicit stalling move
("That's a fair question — let me think for a second") as a legitimate
recovery tool, not a failure.

---

## Lesson 3 (Performance) — Negotiated Decision (presentation-driven decision)

**Purpose:** Present options and then lead a decision-focused discussion
that stays structured even as participants question assumptions and
risks.

**Elicit:** Learner presents 2–3 options, then facilitates a discussion
toward a decision or explicit next steps, managing multiple questioners.

**Listen for:** Whether the learner keeps steering back to a decision
point or lets the discussion drift; whether assumptions get acknowledged
rather than defended reflexively.

**Likely strong-B2 responses:** The discussion wanders across several
tangents with the learner following each one rather than redirecting.

**Upgrade path:** Passive following → active steering: "That's a
related but separate question — let's park it and come back to the
decision in front of us."

**Intervention prompts:**
- "You're following every tangent — bring it back to the decision."
- "Acknowledge that assumption instead of defending it immediately — is
  it actually solid?"
- "Name the decision point again before this drifts further."

**Scorecard focus:** Interaction Control (facilitation, steering),
Discourse Control (maintaining decision focus).

**Extension:** Have a participant deliberately introduce a plausible but
irrelevant tangent to test steering skill.

**Recovery:** Give the learner an explicit parking-lot phrase ("Let's
note that and come back to it") to use whenever they feel discussion
drifting.

---

## Lesson 4 (Model) — Timed Opinion Turn, light framing

**Purpose:** Analyze a strong real-world high-stakes presentation for
storyline, signposting, and Q&A strategy.

**Elicit:** Learners study a model presentation/briefing, identify its
narrative arc and signposting language, then give short spoken
reflections reusing its framing moves on a new topic.

**Listen for/Look for:** Whether learners identify actual signposting
language ("turning to," "the key question here is") or just describe
content ("then they talked about costs").

**Likely strong-B2 responses:** "First they talked about the problem,
then the solution." (content summary, not structural/linguistic
analysis.)

**Upgrade path:** Content summary → structural analysis: "They signal the
shift with 'the real question is' — that's what makes the transition feel
deliberate rather than just sequential."

**Intervention prompts:**
- "That's what they said — now, how did they signal moving from one part
  to the next?"
- "Find the exact phrase that marks a transition."
- "Reuse that phrase in your own 20-second version."

**Scorecard focus:** Discourse Control (signposting language), Advanced
Range (noticing rhetorical framing devices).

**Extension:** Compare two model presentations with different signposting
styles (question-led vs. statement-led) and evaluate which suits which
context.

**Recovery:** Supply a signposting phrase bank if learners can't
self-identify transition language, then have them locate matches in the
model.

---

## Lesson 5 (Model) — Mediation Brief, guided (2–3 presentation examples)

**Purpose:** Compare presentation/briefing examples on similar issues and
brief a peer on differences in tone, structure, and question-handling.

**Elicit:** Two or three presentation examples on a similar
announcement/change; a spoken briefing to a peer on how they differ.

**Listen for/Look for:** Whether differences noted are substantive
(structure, tone, question-handling strategy) or superficial (one is
"more boring").

**Likely strong-B2 responses:** "This one is better because it's more
interesting." (a real judgment with no analytic content behind it.)

**Upgrade path:** Vague preference → structural comparison: "This one
opens with the impact on people, the other opens with numbers — the
people-first version probably lands better with a general audience."

**Intervention prompts:**
- "'More interesting' isn't a reason — what's actually different about
  how they open?"
- "How does each one handle a likely tough question?"
- "Which would you recommend for this specific audience, and why?"

**Scorecard focus:** Precision (substantive comparison criteria),
Discourse Control (structured briefing to a peer).

**Extension:** Add a third example that's structurally strong but
tonally mismatched to the audience.

**Recovery:** Provide a comparison grid (opening move / structure /
tone / Q&A handling) to fill in before speaking.

---

## Lesson 6 (Model) — Negotiated Decision, guided model analysis

**Purpose:** Study how a presenter in a board/steering-committee setting
frames a decision, invites questions, and responds strategically to
pushback.

**Elicit:** A model high-stakes meeting recording/transcript; learners map
how the presenter frames the ask, opens the floor, and handles pushback
without shutting it down.

**Listen for/Look for:** Whether learners notice the specific move of
*inviting* pushback ("What am I missing?") as a deliberate credibility
strategy, not just a passive Q&A moment.

**Likely strong-B2 responses:** "Then people asked questions and the
presenter answered them." (misses that the presenter may have actively
solicited challenge as a strategic move.)

**Upgrade path:** Passive Q&A description → strategic-invitation
noticing: "The presenter asks 'what am I missing' before anyone
objects — that pre-empts pushback and signals confidence."

**Intervention prompts:**
- "Does the presenter wait for questions, or actively invite them? Find
  the moment."
- "Why would inviting pushback before it happens be a smart move here?"
- "Try that move yourself in a 30-second mini-briefing."

**Scorecard focus:** Interaction Control (pre-emptive invitation of
challenge), Pragmatics (projecting confidence through openness).

**Extension:** Have learners identify a moment where the presenter
declines to answer fully and evaluate whether that was the right call.

**Recovery:** If the strategic-invitation move is hard to spot, point
directly to the timestamp/line and ask only "why do this here?"

---

## Lesson 7 (Output) — Mediation Brief → written briefing note/deck outline

**Purpose:** Produce the written scaffold for a high-stakes presentation:
context, stakes, key messages, anticipated questions, decision points.

**Elicit:** A written briefing note or slide outline (structured, not
prose-heavy) covering context, stakes, 2–3 key messages, anticipated
questions, and a decision point.

**Listen for/Look for:** Whether "anticipated questions" are genuinely
anticipated (specific, plausible) or generic filler ("Are there any
questions?").

**Likely strong-B2 responses:** An outline with solid context and
messages but an anticipated-questions section reading "some people might
have questions about cost."

**Upgrade path:** Generic filler → specific anticipation: "Likely
question: why not delay six months instead? Prepared answer: delay risks
missing the Q3 window entirely."

**Intervention prompts:**
- "What's the actual, specific question someone will ask? Not 'questions
  about cost' — the real one."
- "Have you prepared an answer, or just flagged that a question exists?"
- "Pick the toughest likely question and answer it right now."

**Scorecard focus:** Precision (specific anticipated questions and
answers), Discourse Control (outline structure).

**Extension:** Add a second, harder anticipated question from a different
stakeholder perspective.

**Recovery:** If anticipation stays generic, ask "who in the room would
push back hardest, and on what?" as a concrete starting point.

---

## Lesson 8 (Output) — Negotiated Decision → decision-oriented slide sequence/script

**Purpose:** Design a slide sequence or script walking an audience through
situation, options, risks, recommendation, with explicit Q&A/contingency
slots.

**Elicit:** A short structured slide sequence or script (5–7 slide-worth
of content) with explicit built-in points for questions and
contingencies.

**Listen for/Look for:** Whether Q&A/contingency slots are placed
deliberately (after the recommendation, before close) or just tacked onto
the end without integration.

**Likely strong-B2 responses:** A sequence that presents everything and
then has one final slide titled "Questions?" with no other integration.

**Upgrade path:** Tacked-on Q&A → integrated checkpoints: insert a
mid-sequence pause slide after the options ("Before I give my
recommendation — any concerns about these options so far?").

**Intervention prompts:**
- "'Questions?' at the end isn't a strategy — where could you check in
  earlier?"
- "What's your contingency if the room pushes back on the recommendation
  itself?"
- "Build that checkpoint into the sequence, not just at the end."

**Scorecard focus:** Discourse Control (integrated pacing/checkpoints),
Interaction Control (planned audience engagement).

**Extension:** Add a branch: "if the room rejects the recommendation,
what's slide 6?"

**Recovery:** If integration is hard, have the learner mark on their
existing sequence just one place a checkpoint could go, then build out
from there.

---

## Lesson 9 (Output) — Timed Opinion Turn → post-briefing follow-up message

**Purpose:** Write a concise follow-up capturing questions raised,
decisions made, and next steps — proof that Q&A outcomes were actually
captured, not just survived.

**Elicit:** A short follow-up email/summary (100–130 words) after a
high-stakes briefing, naming key questions raised, decisions made, and
next steps with owners.

**Listen for/Look for:** Whether next steps have named owners and
timing, or are left as vague intentions ("we'll look into it").

**Likely strong-B2 responses:** "We'll follow up on the budget question
soon." (no owner, no timing — unusable as an actual record.)

**Upgrade path:** Vague intention → owned action: "Finance will confirm
the budget figure by Friday."

**Intervention prompts:**
- "Who owns that? Name a person or team."
- "'Soon' isn't a timeline — when, specifically?"
- "If someone read only this email, could they act on it? Fix what's
  missing."

**Scorecard focus:** Precision (owner + timing on every action item),
Register Control (concise professional follow-up tone).

**Extension:** Add a line addressing a question that was raised but not
fully answered live.

**Recovery:** Supply the frame "[Owner] will [action] by [date]" for each
next step if the learner struggles to make them concrete.

# Unit 5: Problem-Solving and Negotiation — Teacher Guidance

### Mode order: Model → Output → Performance

## Lesson 1 (Model) — Timed Opinion Turn, light framing

**Purpose:** Notice how a model problem-solving discussion frames problems
and options, before learners attempt this themselves in Lesson 7.

**Elicit:** A model discussion/commentary on solving a practical problem;
learners identify how the problem and options are framed, then give
lighter opinion turns reusing those frames.

**Listen for/Look for:** Whether learners notice framing choices (how a
problem is defined shapes which solutions seem reasonable) or just
summarize the content.

**Likely strong-B2 responses:** "They talk about the problem and then
give some solutions." (accurate but doesn't engage with how the framing
of the problem constrains the solution set.)

**Upgrade path:** Content summary → framing analysis: "By defining the
problem as 'a cost issue,' they automatically rule out solutions that
would cost more, even good ones — that's a framing choice, not a neutral
description."

**Intervention prompts:**
- "How is the problem defined here — and what does that framing rule
  out?"
- "Would a different framing open up different solutions?"
- "Reuse that framing move in your own opinion turn."

**Scorecard focus:** Advanced Range (analyzing framing as rhetorical
choice), Precision (identifying what a frame includes/excludes).

**Extension:** Have learners reframe the same problem a second way and
generate a different solution set from it.

**Recovery:** If framing analysis is too abstract, ask concretely: "If we
called this a 'time problem' instead of a 'cost problem,' what would
change?"

---

## Lesson 2 (Model) — Mediation Brief, guided (2 short sources)

**Purpose:** Summarize two stakeholders' problem descriptions for a
decision-maker who needs the full picture — extends Unit 2's mediation
skill into a problem-solving context.

**Elicit:** Two short stakeholder problem descriptions; a spoken summary
for a decision-maker covering both fully and fairly.

**Listen for:** Whether the summary preserves each stakeholder's actual
priority, or flattens both into a generic "there's a disagreement."

**Likely strong-B2 responses:** "Both sides have different problems and
want different things." (technically true, uselessly vague to an actual
decision-maker.)

**Upgrade path:** Vague flattening → specific dual-priority summary:
"Stakeholder A's real concern is delivery speed; Stakeholder B's is
long-term maintenance cost — those are the two things any solution needs
to address."

**Intervention prompts:**
- "What does each stakeholder actually care about — name it precisely."
- "A decision-maker can't act on 'they want different things' — be
  specific."
- "Say both priorities in one sentence each."

**Scorecard focus:** Precision (naming specific priorities), Discourse
Control (dual-perspective summary structure).

**Extension:** Add a third stakeholder with a priority that conflicts
with both existing ones.

**Recovery:** Ask "if you could only tell the decision-maker one thing
each side cares about, what would it be?" to force specificity.

---

## Lesson 3 (Model) — Negotiated Decision, guided model analysis

**Purpose:** Identify how proposals, concessions, and conditional offers
are sequenced and phrased in a model negotiation — direct prerequisite to
Lesson 9's live negotiation.

**Elicit:** A model negotiation transcript; learners label each move
(propose/concede/conditional offer) and identify the exact phrasing used
for conditional offers ("if X, then I could...").

**Listen for/Look for:** Whether learners can distinguish an
unconditional concession from a conditional offer — a distinction that
matters a great deal in real negotiation.

**Likely strong-B2 responses:** "This person offers something." (doesn't
distinguish whether it's given freely or tied to a condition.)

**Upgrade path:** Undifferentiated "offering" → conditional-vs-unconditional
distinction: "This isn't a free concession — it's conditional: they'll
only do it if the other side also moves on price."

**Intervention prompts:**
- "Is that offer free, or does it come with a condition? Find the exact
  words."
- "What's the 'if... then...' structure here?"
- "Try phrasing your own conditional offer using that structure."

**Scorecard focus:** Precision (conditional vs. unconditional language),
Advanced Range (conditional structures — "were X to happen, I'd be
willing to...").

**Extension:** Have learners rewrite an unconditional concession in the
model as a conditional offer instead, and discuss the strategic
difference.

**Recovery:** If the distinction doesn't land, use a simple test
question: "does this only happen if something else happens too?"

---

## Lesson 4 (Output) — Mediation Brief → written problem summary/options note

**Purpose:** Write a structured problem brief a manager could act on:
situation, key constraints, possible options.

**Elicit:** A written problem brief (120–150 words): situation,
constraints, 2–3 options, no recommendation yet (that's Lesson 5).

**Listen for/Look for:** Whether constraints are stated as real limits
(with consequences if violated) or just listed as facts with no bearing
on the options that follow.

**Likely strong-B2 responses:** A list of options that doesn't visibly
connect to the constraints stated earlier — as if the constraints section
and options section were written independently.

**Upgrade path:** Disconnected sections → constraint-driven options: each
option should visibly respect or explicitly violate a named constraint
("Option B exceeds the budget constraint but meets the deadline").

**Intervention prompts:**
- "Does each option respect the constraints you just listed? Check."
- "If an option breaks a constraint, say so explicitly — don't hide it."
- "Constraints should shape the options, not just sit above them."

**Scorecard focus:** Discourse Control (constraint-to-option
connection), Precision (explicit trade-off flagging).

**Extension:** Add a constraint that eliminates one of the options
entirely, and have the learner note that explicitly.

**Recovery:** Have the learner draw a literal line connecting each
constraint to the option(s) it affects before finalizing prose.

---

## Lesson 5 (Output) — Negotiated Decision → written recommendation/proposal

**Purpose:** Recommend a solution with supporting arguments and
contingency plans — the natural next step from Lesson 4's neutral options
note.

**Elicit:** A written recommendation (130–160 words): preferred solution,
2 supporting arguments, at least one contingency plan.

**Listen for/Look for:** Whether the contingency plan is a real "if this
goes wrong, then..." plan, or a vague reassurance ("we'll monitor
closely").

**Likely strong-B2 responses:** "If there are problems, we will address
them as they come up." (not a contingency plan — a statement of intent to
have a plan later.)

**Upgrade path:** Vague reassurance → real contingency: "If adoption is
below 50% after month one, we'll revert to the previous process while we
investigate."

**Intervention prompts:**
- "'We'll address it' isn't a plan — what would you actually do?"
- "Name a specific trigger and a specific response."
- "Would this contingency actually help someone six months from now?"

**Scorecard focus:** Precision (specific trigger-response contingency),
Discourse Control (argument-then-contingency structure).

**Extension:** Add a second contingency for a different failure mode.

**Recovery:** Supply the frame "If [specific trigger], then we will
[specific action]" if the contingency stays vague.

---

## Lesson 6 (Output) — Timed Opinion Turn → structured argument/decision rationale

**Purpose:** Convert a spoken defense of a chosen solution into a
structured rationale suitable for email or slide outline — closes the
written arc of the unit.

**Elicit:** A written rationale (100–130 words) explaining why the chosen
solution was selected, structured for a reader who wasn't part of the
decision.

**Listen for/Look for:** Whether the rationale explains the *choice*
(why this over the alternatives) or just restates the *solution*
(what it is).

**Likely strong-B2 responses:** A clear description of the solution with
no comparison to what wasn't chosen — reads like a proposal, not a
rationale for a decision already made.

**Upgrade path:** Solution description → comparative rationale: add "...
rather than [alternative], which would have addressed X but not Y."

**Intervention prompts:**
- "This describes the solution — now say why it beat the alternative."
- "What did the alternative solve that this one doesn't, and why was that
  an acceptable trade-off?"
- "A rationale answers 'why this, not that' — does yours?"

**Scorecard focus:** Discourse Control (comparative rationale
structure), Precision (naming the actual trade-off accepted).

**Extension:** Have the learner write the rationale a rejected
alternative's advocate would find hardest to argue with.

**Recovery:** Ask directly: "What was the second-best option, and why
didn't it win?" — the answer is usually the missing sentence.

---

## Lesson 7 (Performance) — Timed Opinion Turn + light Rapid Reformulation

**Purpose:** Take quick live positions on handling practical problems and
refine for clarity and feasibility — moves the unit's analytic work back
into spontaneous speech.

**Elicit:** Quick positions (30–45 seconds) on how to handle a practical
problem, then immediate reformulation focused on feasibility, not just
clarity.

**Listen for:** Whether the reformulated version is more feasible, or
just more fluently worded while remaining just as impractical.

**Likely strong-B2 responses:** "We should just give everyone more
training." (a real position, but no engagement with cost, time, or
whether training actually addresses the root problem.)

**Upgrade path:** Unfeasible-but-fluent → feasibility-tested: "Training
helps, but only if the root issue is skill, not workload — worth checking
that before committing budget to it."

**Intervention prompts:**
- "Is that actually feasible, or just the first thing that comes to
  mind?"
- "What would need to be true for your solution to actually work?"
- "Say it again, addressing the feasibility problem I just raised."

**Scorecard focus:** Precision (feasibility-testing a proposal),
Naturalness (fluent live delivery under refinement pressure).

**Extension:** Introduce a resource constraint (half the budget assumed)
and require the position to be revised live.

**Recovery:** If feasibility-testing is hard live, ask a single
diagnostic question ("what could stop this from working?") before asking
for the reformulation.

---

## Lesson 8 (Performance) — Challenge & Pushback

**Purpose:** Propose solutions and handle pushback on cost, risk, or
fairness — direct rehearsal for Lesson 9's full negotiation.

**Elicit:** Learner proposes a solution; a partner/teacher pushes back
specifically on cost, risk, or fairness grounds; learner justifies or
adapts.

**Listen for:** Whether justification engages the specific objection
raised (cost/risk/fairness) or defaults to a generic defense of the
original idea.

**Likely strong-B2 responses:** Pushback: "That's not fair to the smaller
teams." Response: "I still think it's the best solution overall." (talks
past the fairness objection entirely.)

**Upgrade path:** Talking-past → direct engagement: "You're right that it
puts more burden on smaller teams — here's how I'd adjust it to balance
that."

**Intervention prompts:**
- "You didn't actually respond to the fairness point — address it
  directly."
- "Adapt the proposal, don't just restate it louder."
- "Say what you'd change, specifically, in response to that objection."

**Scorecard focus:** Pragmatics (engaging objections on their own terms),
Interaction Control (adapting a proposal live).

**Extension:** Stack a second, different objection (now on risk) right
after the first is resolved.

**Recovery:** If the learner keeps defaulting to restating the original
proposal, explicitly ask "what would you change to fix that specific
problem?"

---

## Lesson 9 (Performance) — Negotiated Decision (short scenario)

**Purpose:** Run a short, high-pressure negotiation with clear but
conflicting priorities — the unit's full-synthesis closing task.

**Elicit:** A timed negotiation scenario (2–3 minutes) between two
learners with conflicting priorities, aiming for a concrete agreement.

**Listen for:** Whether proposals get more specific over the course of
the negotiation (a sign of real progress) or stay at the same level of
vagueness throughout; whether the final agreement is stated explicitly by
both sides.

**Likely strong-B2 responses:** A negotiation that ends with "okay, I
guess that's fine" with neither side stating what was actually agreed.

**Upgrade path:** Unstated agreement → explicit close: "So to confirm:
we're doing X, with condition Y — agreed?"

**Intervention prompts:**
- "Time's almost up — what exactly have you agreed to?"
- "Say the final agreement out loud, both of you."
- "Is there a condition attached? State it."

**Scorecard focus:** Interaction Control (closing explicitly under time
pressure), Discourse Control (concrete final agreement).

**Extension:** Add a hard deadline (60 seconds left) partway through to
force faster convergence.

**Recovery:** If time runs out with no agreement, have both sides state
"the one thing I need" and build a minimal agreement from those two
statements.

# Unit 6: Register, Nuance, and Diplomatic Control — Teacher Guidance

### Mode order: Output → Performance → Model

## Lesson 1 (Output) — Mediation Brief → written tone/register note

**Purpose:** Write a short analytic note explaining how two messages
differ in stance, tone, or register, and what would suit a target
audience better — the unit's diagnostic entry point.

**Elicit:** Two contrasting messages on the same topic (one blunt, one
tactful); a written note (100–130 words) analyzing the tonal difference
and recommending which suits a stated audience.

**Listen for/Look for:** Whether the analysis names specific linguistic
features (hedges, modal choice, directness) or stays at the level of
"one is nicer than the other."

**Likely strong-B2 responses:** "Message A sounds nicer and message B
sounds rude." (a real judgment, but with no linguistic evidence.)

**Upgrade path:** Vague judgment → feature-based analysis: "Message A
hedges the refusal with 'I'm afraid we're not able to at this time,'
while B states it as a flat 'no' — that hedging is what reads as more
tactful."

**Intervention prompts:**
- "What word or phrase makes A sound 'nicer'? Point to it."
- "Is it the content that's different, or just how it's phrased?"
- "Name the specific hedge or softener being used."

**Scorecard focus:** Precision (identifying specific tonal features),
Register Control (matching register to a named audience).

**Extension:** Provide a third message that's over-softened to the point
of being unclear, and have the learner critique that failure mode too.

**Recovery:** Give a short feature checklist (hedging, directness,
personal pronouns, modal verbs) to scan for if analysis stays vague.

---

## Lesson 2 (Output) — Negotiated Decision → written rewrite/recommendation

**Purpose:** Rewrite a message so a decision, refusal, or condition is
phrased with more tact, precision, and audience control.

**Elicit:** A blunt draft message (a refusal, a condition, a difficult
decision); a rewritten version (matching length, roughly) with improved
tact and precision.

**Listen for/Look for:** Whether the rewrite actually preserves the
original content and commitments (tact shouldn't blur the message) while
softening delivery.

**Likely strong-B2 responses:** A rewrite so softened that the actual
decision becomes unclear — tact achieved at the cost of the message
itself.

**Upgrade path:** Over-softened/unclear → tactful-and-clear: keep the
decision explicit ("we won't be able to extend the deadline") while
softening only the framing around it, not the fact itself.

**Intervention prompts:**
- "Is the actual decision still clear in your rewrite? Check."
- "You softened it so much I can't tell what you're actually saying —
  fix that."
- "Tact changes the framing, not the content — keep the content intact."

**Scorecard focus:** Register Control (tact without ambiguity),
Pragmatics (face-saving while preserving message clarity).

**Extension:** Have the learner rewrite the same message for a second,
more senior audience requiring even more careful framing.

**Recovery:** If clarity is lost, have the learner first state the bare
decision in one blunt sentence, then add tact only around that fixed
core.

---

## Lesson 3 (Output) — Timed Opinion Turn → structured reflection/register-shift task

**Purpose:** Convert a spoken response into a piece showing deliberate
register shifting — formalizing an informal point or softening an overly
direct one.

**Elicit:** A written piece (120–150 words) that takes an informal spoken
stance and deliberately reworks it into a more formal register (or vice
versa), then reflects briefly on what changed.

**Listen for/Look for:** Whether the reflection identifies actual
linguistic changes (contractions removed, hedges added, vocabulary
formalized) or just says "I made it more formal."

**Likely strong-B2 responses:** "I changed some words to make it sound
more professional." (true but not specific enough to demonstrate real
control.)

**Upgrade path:** Vague self-report → specific linguistic
accounting: "I removed contractions, replaced 'a lot of people think' with
'there is broad consensus that,' and added a hedge before the strongest
claim."

**Intervention prompts:**
- "Name three specific changes you made, not just 'more formal.'"
- "What's one word or phrase you specifically swapped, and why that
  swap?"
- "Could you do the reverse shift just as precisely?"

**Scorecard focus:** Register Control (demonstrated, not just claimed),
Advanced Range (formal lexis, nominalisation, hedging).

**Extension:** Have the learner do a third version at an intermediate
register, between the two extremes.

**Recovery:** If specific accounting is hard, have the learner highlight
the changed words/phrases directly on the page before describing them.

---

## Lesson 4 (Performance) — Timed Opinion Turn + light Rapid Reformulation

**Purpose:** Respond quickly to sensitive prompts, then immediately
reformulate to adjust strength, tact, certainty, or interpersonal tone.

**Elicit:** Quick live responses to sensitive prompts (declining a
request, raising a concern with a superior), then immediate
reformulation on a specified dimension (softer / more certain / more
tactful).

**Listen for:** Whether the reformulation actually shifts along the
requested dimension, or just rephrases without changing the underlying
register.

**Likely strong-B2 responses:** Asked to make it "softer," the learner
adds "sorry" or "maybe" without otherwise changing the sentence — a
surface fix, not a real register shift.

**Upgrade path:** Surface softening → structural softening: replace a
direct statement with a hedged/indirect structure ("I wonder if we might
reconsider..." instead of "I don't agree, sorry").

**Intervention prompts:**
- "Adding 'sorry' isn't the same as softening the structure — try a
  different sentence shape."
- "Say it again using 'I wonder if...' or 'it might be worth...'"
- "Now make that same sentence more direct again — show me you can go
  both ways."

**Scorecard focus:** Naturalness (structural, not just lexical,
softening), Advanced Range (indirect/hedged structures).

**Extension:** Chain three reformulations in a row, each on a different
requested dimension.

**Recovery:** Supply 2–3 structural softening frames ("I wonder if...",
"it might be worth considering...", "one option could be...") to plug
into on request.

---

## Lesson 5 (Performance) — Challenge & Pushback

**Purpose:** Defend or soften a position under pressure while practicing
hedging, diplomatic disagreement, face-saving, and tone recalibration.

**Elicit:** Learner holds a position under sustained challenge in a
sensitive scenario (disagreeing with a superior, declining a client
request), required to stay diplomatic throughout.

**Listen for:** Whether diplomatic tone holds under sustained (not just
single) pressure — many learners manage one polite turn but crack by the
second or third challenge.

**Likely strong-B2 responses:** Polite first response, then by the third
challenge: "Look, I already said no." (diplomatic control breaks down
under sustained pressure — a realistic and expected pattern.)

**Upgrade path:** Breakdown under sustained pressure → maintained
control: "I understand this is frustrating, and I want to help where I
can, but the answer on this specific point hasn't changed."

**Intervention prompts:**
- "You held it together for one turn — the third challenge is where it
  matters. Try again."
- "Acknowledge the frustration without abandoning the position."
- "What's a phrase you can return to each time, so you don't run out of
  diplomatic language?"

**Scorecard focus:** Pragmatics (sustained face-saving), Interaction
Control (holding a position across multiple challenge rounds).

**Extension:** Extend the challenge to five rounds instead of three to
stress-test sustained control.

**Recovery:** Give the learner one reusable "anchor phrase" ("I hear you,
and I want to find a way to help — but on this point specifically...")
they can return to whenever they feel control slipping.

---

## Lesson 6 (Performance) — Negotiated Decision (short scenario)

**Purpose:** Work through a decision task where the challenge is not the
content but how to phrase conditions, disagreement, and compromise
appropriately — the unit's core "form over substance" focus.

**Elicit:** A short decision task where the substantive answer is
relatively easy, but the scenario requires careful phrasing (e.g.
delivering unwelcome news to a valued client while preserving the
relationship).

**Listen for:** Whether the learner focuses on getting the phrasing right
under the assumption the content is simple, or gets distracted trying to
solve the (easy) content problem instead.

**Likely strong-B2 responses:** A clear, correct decision delivered in
blunt, undiplomatic phrasing — content is fine, form isn't.

**Upgrade path:** Correct-but-blunt → correct-and-diplomatic: keep the
decision, add relationship-preserving framing ("we value this
partnership, which is exactly why I want to be upfront that...").

**Intervention prompts:**
- "The decision is fine — it's how you said it that needs work."
- "How would you say this if you needed this client again next year?"
- "Add one sentence that protects the relationship without changing the
  decision."

**Scorecard focus:** Register Control (form independent of content
difficulty), Pragmatics (relationship-preserving framing).

**Extension:** Repeat with a scenario where the content actually is hard,
to contrast "phrasing is the challenge" vs. "everything is the
challenge."

**Recovery:** If the learner fixates on the content, explicitly say "the
decision is already right — focus only on how you say it" to redirect
attention.

---

## Lesson 7 (Model) — Timed Opinion Turn, light framing

**Purpose:** Analyze model examples for stance-marking, hedging, tact,
emphasis, understatement, and formality shifts.

**Elicit:** Model examples rich in diplomatic language; learners identify
specific devices (hedges, understatement, emphasis markers) before giving
short framed responses reusing those moves.

**Listen for/Look for:** Whether learners can name the device (not just
its effect) — e.g. identifying "somewhat" as a downtoner, not just saying
"it sounds less strong."

**Likely strong-B2 responses:** "This sentence sounds less certain."
(correct effect, no identification of the actual device producing it.)

**Upgrade path:** Effect-noticing → device-naming: "'Somewhat' and 'to
some extent' are downtoners — they're what make the claim sound less
certain, without changing the claim itself."

**Intervention prompts:**
- "What word is doing that work? Name it."
- "Could you get the same effect with a different word or phrase?"
- "Use that same device in your own response now."

**Scorecard focus:** Advanced Range (naming and using hedging/downtoning
devices), Precision (device-level analysis).

**Extension:** Have learners build a short personal reference list of
downtoners/boosters they've identified across the unit.

**Recovery:** Provide a short glossary of common hedging/downtoning
devices to match against the model text if identification stalls.

---

## Lesson 8 (Model) — Mediation Brief, guided (2 short sources)

**Purpose:** Compare two texts/voices differing in tone or implied
stance and brief a peer on how they differ not just in content but in
register and interpersonal effect.

**Elicit:** Two texts on the same topic with different implied stances; a
spoken briefing to a peer covering both content and interpersonal/register
differences.

**Listen for/Look for:** Whether the briefing addresses register/stance
at all, or collapses into a content-only summary (a recurring risk once
content is present and salient).

**Likely strong-B2 responses:** A briefing that accurately compares what
each text says but never mentions how each one says it.

**Upgrade path:** Content-only → content-plus-register: add "...and
beyond the content, Text A comes across as more confident/certain, while
Text B hedges more and leaves room for disagreement."

**Intervention prompts:**
- "You've covered what they say — now, how do they say it differently?"
- "Which one sounds more certain? What makes it sound that way?"
- "Brief me on tone specifically, separate from content."

**Scorecard focus:** Register Control (explicit content/tone
separation), Discourse Control (dual-layer briefing structure).

**Extension:** Add a third text with a tone that contradicts its actual
content (confident phrasing masking a weak claim) as a harder case.

**Recovery:** If register keeps getting skipped, ask a forced-choice
question first: "which one sounds more certain?" before asking for the
full briefing.

---

## Lesson 9 (Model) — Negotiated Decision, guided model analysis

**Purpose:** Notice how speakers soften claims, signal caution, disagree
tactfully, and reframe sensitive points without sounding vague or
weak — the unit's and track's closing synthesis point.

**Elicit:** A model interaction requiring diplomatic navigation of
disagreement; learners map softening/caution/tactful-disagreement/reframing
moves and evaluate whether any moment tips into sounding weak rather than
tactful.

**Listen for/Look for:** Whether learners can identify the line between
tactful and weak — a genuinely subtle C1/C2 judgment, not just spotting
softening language in general.

**Likely strong-B2 responses:** "This person is very polite." (notices
politeness broadly but doesn't evaluate whether it crosses into
weakness at any point.)

**Upgrade path:** Broad politeness-noticing → precision judgment: "Here,
the hedge is doing real work — protecting the relationship while still
making the point. But here, three hedges in one sentence start to bury
the actual point — that's where it tips into sounding weak."

**Intervention prompts:**
- "Is this tactful, or has it become vague? Where's the line?"
- "Find a moment where hedging helps, and one where it's too much."
- "Rewrite the over-hedged moment so it's tactful but still clear."

**Scorecard focus:** Pragmatics (calibrating tact vs. weakness), Advanced
Range (controlled vs. excessive hedging).

**Extension:** Have learners find or construct their own example of
hedging that tips into weakness, as a diagnostic exercise for their own
future writing/speech.

**Recovery:** If the tactful/weak distinction is elusive, use a concrete
test: "if you removed one more hedge, would the point still be safe to
make? if you added one more, would it disappear?" — that boundary-testing
usually locates the line.
