# Adults C2 Curriculum

All five domains of the Adults C2 track, each a 9-lesson unit built from
three 3-lesson mode blocks (Performance, Model, Output), continuing directly
from Adults C1. Adults C2 has five domains rather than six because C1's
"Problem-Solving and Negotiation" domain was deliberately folded into
"Strategic Diplomacy, Tone, and Stance Management" at C2 — not dropped, but
compressed, since C2 prioritizes refinement and mastery over topic coverage.

Each unit uses only the five canonical Speaking Simulator patterns, shared
with Adults C1 and Teens C1.

Units use the validated 5-unit rotation, which cannot distribute three mode
sequences perfectly evenly across five units — the resulting asymmetry is
deliberate, not an error: each mode is short one occurrence in a different
structural position (Output as opener, Performance in the middle, Model as
closer), while total mode presence across the course stays exactly balanced
(Performance=5, Model=5, Output=5 appearances across all 15 mode-block
slots).

| Unit | Domain | Mode order |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | High-Stakes Argument and Rebuttal | Performance → Model → Output |
| 2 | Advanced Mediation and Synthesis | Model → Output → Performance |
| 3 | Executive Writing and Concise Professional Communication | Output → Performance → Model |
| 4 | High-Impact Presentation, Briefing, and Q&A | Performance → Model → Output |
| 5 | Strategic Diplomacy, Tone, and Stance Management | Model → Output → Performance |

Every unit is deliberately more Strategic-band than its Adults C1
counterpart — higher stakes (organisational, civic, reputational
consequences), denser/more technical input, and language used to shape
outcomes (calibrated concession, audience control, rhetorical economy)
rather than just complete the task. Unit 3 in particular is explicitly not
a revived version of C1's dropped "Problem-Solving and Negotiation" domain —
it centers on compression and economy in executive writing, not negotiation.

This is content/planning-stage only — none of it is built into the app yet
(no Supabase rows, no slide-type mapping, no images/audio).

# Unit 1: High-Stakes Argument and Rebuttal

### Mode order: Performance → Model → Output

| Lesson | Dominant mode | Simulator patterns | Framing |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Performance | Timed Opinion Turn + light Rapid Reformulation | Learners take a position on a genuinely consequential issue, such as a policy decision or organisational change, with very limited preparation time, then immediately refine the stance for precision, control, and rhetorical impact. |
| 2 | Performance | Challenge & Pushback | Learners defend their position against sustained, well-informed objections from a stakeholder or expert who can cite data, alternative frameworks, or risk exposure; focus is on strategic rebuttal, selective concession, and reframing. |
| 3 | Performance | Negotiated Decision (short scenario) | Learners work toward a decision in a scenario where the argument outcome has real consequences, such as resource reallocation or cancellation of a project, balancing persuasive structure with relationship and reputation considerations. |
| 4 | Model | Timed Opinion Turn (light, around model input) | Analyse a high-quality C2-level argumentative text or talk, such as an op-ed, boardroom pitch, or expert panel clip, for stance, argument architecture, and nuance; learners then give shorter framed turns that intentionally reuse those strategies. |
| 5 | Model | Mediation Brief (guided, 2–3 sources) | Compare two or three serious sources, such as reports, data summaries, or expert views, and practise explaining them fairly to a decision-maker, highlighting where arguments align, conflict, or talk past each other. |
| 6 | Model | Negotiated Decision (guided model analysis) | Study a model high-stakes discussion, such as an executive meeting or academic panel, and map how speakers build and sequence arguments, invite counterviews, and steer toward conclusions without shutting down disagreement. |
| 7 | Output | Mediation Brief → written synthesis / decision note | Learners produce a C2-level written synthesis that integrates multiple arguments and evidence, explicitly signals strengths and limitations, and recommends a course of action for a senior audience. |
| 8 | Output | Negotiated Decision → written argument / proposal section | Learners write a proposal or position-paper section that presents a tightly structured argument geared to persuade a critical audience, including anticipated counterarguments and pre-emptive rebuttal. |
| 9 | Output | Timed Opinion Turn → strategic briefing / slide outline | Learners convert their spoken stance into a concise strategic briefing or slide outline for a high-stakes meeting, with clear narrative, rhetorical framing, and call to action. |

# Unit 2: Advanced Mediation and Synthesis

### Mode order: Model → Output → Performance

| Lesson | Dominant mode | Simulator patterns | Framing |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Model | Mediation Brief (guided, 2–3 complex sources) | Learners analyse 2–3 dense, high-stakes texts (e.g. technical reports, policy memos, expert articles) and map purposes, audiences, and viewpoints before speaking, focusing on what must be preserved and what can be simplified for non-expert decision-makers. |
| 2 | Model | Timed Opinion Turn (light, around model input) | Learners give short, neutral-to-lightly-advisory turns explaining the core of a complex issue to a non-expert audience, explicitly avoiding bias while signalling key implications and uncertainties. |
| 3 | Model | Negotiated Decision (guided model analysis) | Learners study a model mediation meeting (e.g. cross-functional crisis review) and trace how the mediator structures information, balances perspectives, and sequences options to keep dialogue constructive under pressure. |
| 4 | Output | Mediation Brief → written synthesis / briefing note | Learners produce a written synthesis that integrates multiple complex sources into a clear briefing for a specific audience (board, regulator, cross-functional team), explicitly flagging what is known, unknown, and contested. |
| 5 | Output | Negotiated Decision → written options & risk summary | Learners write an options-and-risk summary that sets out competing proposals, associated risks, stakeholder positions, and a balanced recommendation, suitable for formal circulation. |
| 6 | Output | Timed Opinion Turn → audience-specific explanation piece | Learners convert a neutral spoken explanation into two short written versions for different audiences (e.g. technical team vs executive summary), highlighting register shifts and choice of what to foreground. |
| 7 | Performance | Mediation Brief (full, spoken under time pressure) | Learners mediate between parties who cannot understand each other directly (e.g. technical team and non-technical leadership, or multilingual stakeholders), under strict time limits, ensuring both sides feel accurately represented and understood. |
| 8 | Performance | Negotiated Decision (mediated decision meeting) | Learners act as mediator in a simulated decision meeting, managing turn-taking, clarifying misunderstandings, and guiding parties toward either a decision or a clearly articulated set of remaining disagreements and next steps. |
| 9 | Performance | Timed Opinion Turn + Rapid Reformulation (mediator's recommendation) | Learners give a short spoken recommendation that explicitly acknowledges all sides, then immediately reformulate to improve perceived neutrality, clarity of trade-offs, and persuasiveness for a mixed audience. |

# Unit 3: Executive Writing and Concise Professional Communication

### Mode order: Output → Performance → Model

| Lesson | Dominant mode | Simulator patterns | Framing |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Output | Mediation Brief → written executive summary | Learners produce a short executive summary of a complex situation (e.g., multi-project status or risk landscape) aimed at senior leadership; focus is on ruthless selection of what matters and clear signalling of priorities. |
| 2 | Output | Negotiated Decision → written options & recommendation note | Learners write a compressed options-and-recommendation note that lays out 2–3 realistic courses of action, evaluates them briefly, and recommends one, assuming the reader is busy and sceptical. |
| 3 | Output | Timed Opinion Turn → concise position paragraph/email | Learners convert a spoken stance on a contentious decision into a single concise paragraph or short email that captures position, key reasons, and implied next steps without padding. |
| 4 | Performance | Timed Opinion Turn + light Rapid Reformulation | Learners orally defend one of their written recommendations to a senior audience who has only skimmed the note; they then immediately reformulate to tighten phrasing and better mirror what that audience cares about (risk, cost, optics, timing). |
| 5 | Performance | Challenge & Pushback | Learners present a compressed written recommendation and handle pointed questions such as "why didn't you mention X?", "what evidence underlies this?", or "what happens if this fails?", practising concise, non-defensive justification rather than long explanations. |
| 6 | Performance | Negotiated Decision (short scenario) | Learners discuss a scenario where written recommendations from different stakeholders conflict and must be reconciled; they practise arguing for or refining their written positions in live discussion while keeping references to the written documents clear and economical. |
| 7 | Model | Timed Opinion Turn (light, around model input) | Learners analyse exemplary executive-level texts (e.g., one-page memos, high-quality leadership emails, compressed board papers) for structure, density, stance, and what gets omitted; they then give short spoken reflections on why these texts work, reusing observed patterns. |
| 8 | Model | Mediation Brief (guided, 2–3 exemplary texts) | Learners compare two or three strong examples of concise professional communication (e.g., different executives writing about similar issues) and brief a peer on how they differ in density, tone, and prioritisation, identifying which is better suited to which audience. |
| 9 | Model | Negotiated Decision (guided model analysis) | Learners study a decision process where written briefs play a central role (e.g., meeting packet plus live discussion) and map how concise writing sets the frame, what gets picked up or ignored in the meeting, and how written follow-up captures the decision for future readers. |

# Unit 4: High-Impact Presentation, Briefing, and Q&A

### Mode order: Performance → Model → Output

| Lesson | Dominant mode | Simulator patterns | Framing |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Performance | Timed Opinion Turn + light Rapid Reformulation | Learners deliver a very short "stand-up" briefing or mini-presentation on a high-stakes topic (e.g., major change initiative, critical incident, board-visible project) and immediately reformulate to sharpen narrative, audience framing, and call-to-action. |
| 2 | Performance | Challenge & Pushback (Q&A-under-challenge) | After a brief presentation or briefing, learners face unscripted, potentially hostile or sceptical Q&A from powerful stakeholders (senior leadership, external partners). Focus is on handling tough questions concisely, maintaining credibility and composure, and adjusting stance without over-explaining. |
| 3 | Performance | Negotiated Decision (presentation-driven decision) | Learners present options or a recommendation and then lead a decision-focused discussion where participants question assumptions, risks, and impacts; they must keep the conversation structured and move toward a decision or clearly articulated next steps. |
| 4 | Model | Timed Opinion Turn (light, around model input) | Learners analyse a strong real-world high-stakes presentation or briefing (e.g., investor pitch, CEO town hall, crisis briefing) for storyline, signposting, and Q&A strategy, then give short spoken reflections on what makes it effective, reusing its framing moves. |
| 5 | Model | Mediation Brief (guided, 2–3 presentation examples) | Learners compare two or three presentation/briefing examples addressing similar issues (e.g., two different leaders announcing a major change) and brief a peer on how they differ in tone, structure, and handling of questions or concerns. |
| 6 | Model | Negotiated Decision (guided model analysis) | Learners study a recorded or scripted high-stakes meeting where a presentation leads into discussion and decision (e.g., board meeting, senior steering committee) and map how the presenter frames the decision, invites questions, and responds strategically to pushback. |
| 7 | Output | Mediation Brief → written briefing note / deck outline | Learners produce a written briefing note or slide outline that sets up a high-stakes presentation: clear context, stakes, key messages, anticipated questions, and time-boxed decision points, designed for a specific decision-making audience. |
| 8 | Output | Negotiated Decision → decision-oriented slide sequence / script | Learners design a short sequence of slides or speaking script that walks an audience through situation, options, risks, and recommendation, with explicit slots for Q&A and contingency discussion. |
| 9 | Output | Timed Opinion Turn → post-briefing follow-up message | Learners write a concise follow-up email or summary after a high-stakes presentation/briefing that acknowledges key questions raised, clarifies decisions made, and outlines next steps and responsibilities, demonstrating that Q&A outcomes are captured and communicated clearly. |

# Unit 5: Strategic Diplomacy, Tone, and Stance Management

### Mode order: Model → Output → Performance

| Lesson | Dominant mode | Simulator patterns | Framing |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Model | Mediation Brief (guided, 2–3 delicate communications) | Learners analyse 2–3 high-stakes "delicate" texts (e.g., crisis statements, difficult internal emails, diplomatic notes) for audience, purpose, implied stance, and what is left unsaid; focus is on how tone protects relationships, reputation, or negotiations. |
| 2 | Model | Timed Opinion Turn (light, around model input) | Learners give short, analytical turns explaining how a given message manages tone and stance (e.g., how it admits fault, expresses empathy, or minimises alarm) without quoting the text verbatim; they practise naming specific linguistic choices that make it diplomatic. |
| 3 | Model | Negotiated Decision (guided model analysis) | Learners study a model interaction where diplomatic language is used to navigate disagreement (e.g., leadership conflict, cross-team tension, or international dispute) and map how speakers rephrase, hedge, and reframe to keep dialogue open while signalling boundaries. |
| 4 | Output | Mediation Brief → written tone/stance redesign | Learners rewrite a problematic or overly direct message (email, announcement, statement) for a specific audience, preserving the underlying facts while changing tone, stance, and emphasis to reduce damage and improve alignment. |
| 5 | Output | Negotiated Decision → multi-audience message set | Learners produce two short versions of the same core message for different audiences (e.g., internal staff vs external partners, senior leadership vs public), showing controlled shifts in directness, formality, and stance while keeping the core commitments consistent. |
| 6 | Output | Timed Opinion Turn → written "diplomatic response" templates | Learners convert spoken diplomatic responses (e.g., how to say "no," deliver bad news, or question a decision tactfully) into reusable written templates, capturing hedges, qualifiers, and framing moves that soften impact without obscuring the message. |
| 7 | Performance | Timed Opinion Turn + Rapid Reformulation (live stance-shift) | Learners respond in real time to sensitive prompts (disagreement with a senior person, pushback from a partner, complaint from a client) and immediately reformulate to adjust strength, tact, and stance, practising moving from "too blunt / too vague" to strategically calibrated. |
| 8 | Performance | Challenge & Pushback (high-stakes relational scenarios) | Learners handle sustained, emotionally charged challenge in scenarios where relationships or reputation are at stake (e.g., being blamed for a problem, pushed to take a risky public stance); focus is on keeping dialogue constructive, protecting face on all sides, and signalling boundaries without escalation. |
| 9 | Performance | Negotiated Decision (diplomatic resolution or non-resolution) | Learners work through a short diplomatic negotiation (internal or external) where full agreement may not be possible; they practise articulating partial agreement, "agreeing to disagree," or proposing next steps in language that maintains working relationships and preserves future options. |
