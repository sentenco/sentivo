# Teens C1 Curriculum

All six domains of the Teens C1 track, each a 9-lesson unit built from
three 3-lesson mode blocks (Performance, Model, Output), sharing the C1/C2
framework and the identical six-unit mode-rotation table validated for
Adults C1. Every unit uses only the five canonical Speaking Simulator
patterns (frozen vocabulary shared with Adults C1 and Adults C2), with
teen-appropriate scenarios layered on top rather than teen-specific pattern
names.

**Unit numbering reflects build order, not the original semantic domain
list.** The original planning list ordered domains as: 1. Argument, 2.
Mediation, 3. Identity, 4. Presentations, 5. Peer Negotiation, 6. Register.
During actual construction, each domain was chosen based on the strongest
pedagogical bridge from the unit just completed, which produced a different
—and, on review, better—sequence. That build-order sequence was deliberately
adopted as the final published order, because it preserves four confirmed,
real inter-unit bridges that the original semantic order would have broken
for no structural benefit (the six rotation slots are used exactly once
either way). Ending on Identity, Social Issues, and Self-Expression also
works well as a reflective capstone, synthesizing argument, mediation,
audience-control, and nuance in personally meaningful but carefully bounded
tasks.

| Unit | Domain | Mode order | Bridge from previous unit |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Argument, Debate, and Respectful Disagreement | Performance → Model → Output | — (opens the track) |
| 2 | Mediation, Source Comparison, and Summaries | Model → Output → Performance | Unit 1's closing lesson used Mediation Brief to reconstruct opposing viewpoints |
| 3 | Presentations, School Talks, and Discussions | Output → Performance → Model | Unit 2's closing Performance lessons trained explaining/adapting for one listener under pressure |
| 4 | Register, Tone, and Audience Awareness | Performance → Model → Output | Unit 3's closing lesson analyzed how presenters adapt tone/detail for different audiences |
| 5 | Peer Negotiation and Problem-Solving | Model → Output → Performance | Unit 4 already had learners weighing formality/directness when phrasing disagreement |
| 6 | Identity, Social Issues, and Self-Expression | Output → Performance → Model | Capstone: synthesizes stance, mediation, audience control, and nuance (weaker direct bridge, chosen for capstone fit) |

This is content/planning-stage only — none of it is built into the app yet
(no Supabase rows, no slide-type mapping, no images/audio).

# Unit 1: Argument, Debate, and Respectful Disagreement

### Mode order: Performance → Model → Output

| Lesson | Dominant mode | Simulator patterns | Framing |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Performance | Timed Opinion Turn | Students give short but developed responses to "big questions" that matter to teens, such as whether schools should limit phone use, whether homework improves learning, or whether online anonymity helps honest self-expression; the target is a sustained C1 turn, not a one-sentence opinion. |
| 2 | Performance | Challenge & Pushback | One learner defends a position while classmates challenge it with sceptical but non-hostile follow-ups; topics stay teen-relevant and debatable, and the emphasis is on calm defence, clarification, and respectful disagreement rather than "winning." |
| 3 | Performance | Negotiated Decision | Small groups must reach a decision on a school or youth issue, such as setting fair class rules, allocating budget for a student event, or choosing guidelines for AI use in assignments; learners must weigh trade-offs, make concessions, and justify a final group stance. |
| 4 | Model | Timed Opinion Turn | Students analyse strong model responses to teen-relevant prompts, identifying what makes them sound C1: clear line of argument, developed reasoning, controlled hedging, and spontaneous but organised delivery. |
| 5 | Model | Rapid Reformulation | Learners study how a weak or too blunt response can be reformulated into a stronger one by adding nuance, softening overclaiming, and improving precision; the focus is on moving beyond B2-style "I think X because Y" into more flexible and layered argument. |
| 6 | Model | Challenge & Pushback | Students examine model exchanges where speakers disagree firmly but respectfully, noticing turn-taking, acknowledgement of the other side, evidence use, and language that depersonalises conflict instead of escalating it. |
| 7 | Output | Timed Opinion Turn + Rapid Reformulation | Learners plan and record or deliver a developed stance on a teen issue, then revise it after feedback or new counter-arguments; the output shows whether they can strengthen structure, qualification, and responsiveness rather than just repeat the same opinion. |
| 8 | Output | Negotiated Decision | Students produce a written or slide-based group position on a contested issue, such as a class media policy or a proposal for school change, capturing the reasoning behind the decision and acknowledging counter-arguments. |
| 9 | Output | Mediation Brief | Learners read or hear contrasting teen viewpoints on a current issue and write or present a neutral summary for peers who did not follow the discussion, reconstructing the main arguments clearly before stating where they personally stand. |

# Unit 2: Mediation, Source Comparison, and Summaries

### Mode order: Model → Output → Performance

| Lesson | Dominant mode | Simulator patterns | Framing |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Model | Mediation Brief | Learners analyse short examples where one teen explains a complex or dense text (article, school notice, video transcript) to a friend who would struggle with it; focus is on how key information is selected, simplified, and reorganised without changing the meaning. |
| 2 | Model | Mediation Brief | Students examine side-by-side summaries of the same source (one weak, one strong) and identify what makes the stronger one C1-like: clearer main idea, logical order, significant compression, and accurate representation of stance and tone. |
| 3 | Model | Negotiated Decision | Groups study a model discussion where teens compare two or three sources on the same topic (e.g., different articles or videos about a social issue) and decide which is most reliable or useful; they map how reasons, evidence, and criteria (bias, date, author, audience) are brought into the decision. |
| 4 | Output | Mediation Brief | Learners produce a written or spoken mediation brief explaining a teacher-level or news text in teen-friendly language for classmates (e.g., summarising a school policy email, a science article, or a community notice), highlighting what matters for their peers and omitting detail that does not. |
| 5 | Output | Negotiated Decision | Groups create a short comparison note or slide deck explaining which of two or three sources they would recommend to a friend and why, capturing key differences in reliability, tone, and usefulness in a way that another student could follow without reading the originals. |
| 6 | Output | Timed Opinion Turn | Students record or deliver a concise, structured explanation of "what X is about and why it matters" (for example, a video they watched in class or an article from homework), demonstrating that they can reconstruct the main argument or story spontaneously for an absent classmate. |
| 7 | Performance | Mediation Brief | In real-time, learners receive a short text or infographic and must explain it to a partner who cannot see it, adapting explanations if the partner signals confusion; emphasis is on clarifying, checking understanding, and adjusting language on the fly. |
| 8 | Performance | Negotiated Decision | Small groups receive different snippets or perspectives on the same issue (e.g., different stakeholders' views on a school decision) and must agree on a joint "neutral explanation" for a wider audience, reconciling overlaps and contradictions as they speak. |
| 9 | Performance | Timed Opinion Turn + Rapid Reformulation | Learners first give a short explanation of a complex issue to a peer; after feedback or a follow-up question ("I still don't get X"), they immediately reformulate, improving structure, examples, and clarity while avoiding simplifications that change meaning. |

# Unit 3: Presentations, School Talks, and Discussions

### Mode order: Output → Performance → Model

| Lesson | Dominant mode | Simulator patterns | Framing |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Output | Timed Opinion Turn | Learners plan and rehearse a short, structured school-talk style presentation (e.g., a mini-talk on a personal interest, project, or social issue), focusing on clear introductions, signposting, and conclusions; delivery is still semi-planned, but emphasis is on moving from script to notes, not reading aloud. |
| 2 | Output | Mediation Brief | Students design a brief "explain this for our class" talk based on a text or video (e.g., science article, news story, or school announcement), deciding what to include, how to sequence it, and where to anticipate likely questions from classmates or the teacher. |
| 3 | Output | Negotiated Decision | Small groups co-plan a joint presentation (e.g., a project pitch or class report), negotiating roles, sub-topics, and who covers which part; they produce an outline and notes that explicitly include time for questions or a short discussion segment. |
| 4 | Performance | Timed Opinion Turn + Challenge & Pushback | Learners deliver their short talks to the class and then immediately field unscripted questions from peers and/or the teacher; the focus is on staying calm, clarifying, admitting uncertainty, and extending answers without just repeating prepared lines. |
| 5 | Performance | Negotiated Decision | After a set of mini-talks on related topics (e.g., different solutions to a school issue or different aspects of a theme), groups hold a guided discussion to decide which ideas to prioritise in a shared class recommendation, practising turn-taking and responding to others' points in real time. |
| 6 | Performance | Challenge & Pushback | In a "Q&A focus" session, some learners act as presenters and others as questioners; questioners are coached to ask probing but respectful questions, while presenters practise listening fully, restating questions, and answering specifically, including when they need to say they do not know. |
| 7 | Model | Timed Opinion Turn | Students watch or read model teen presentations (authentic or adapted) and analyse how the speaker structures the talk (signposting, examples, pacing), how formal or informal their language is, and how they make their opinions clear without sounding aggressive or flat. |
| 8 | Model | Challenge & Pushback | Learners study model Q&A sequences after a presentation (video or transcript), identifying strategies the presenter uses: clarifying questions, admitting limits, linking answers back to main points, and handling unexpected or critical questions without losing composure. |
| 9 | Model | Mediation Brief | Students examine how a strong presenter adapts content and explanations for a specific audience (e.g., younger students vs classmates vs teachers), mapping changes in detail, examples, and tone; they connect this to earlier mediation work by noticing how explanation and presentation skills overlap. |

# Unit 4: Register, Tone, and Audience Awareness

### Mode order: Performance → Model → Output

| Lesson | Dominant mode | Simulator patterns | Framing |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Performance | Timed Opinion Turn | Learners respond in real time to prompts requiring quick tone shifts for the same core message (e.g., explaining a problem to a friend vs to a teacher vs in a short email), practising changing formality and phrasing while keeping the underlying meaning intact. |
| 2 | Performance | Rapid Reformulation | Students first give an intentionally "off-tone" response (too blunt, too casual, too formal) in a short speaking turn, then immediately reformulate it to better fit a specified audience and situation (e.g., from chatty to respectful, from vague to precise, from stiff to natural). |
| 3 | Performance | Challenge & Pushback | In pairs, one learner plays a "misreading the situation" role (using inappropriate tone or register in a sensitive context), while the other challenges them and helps reformulate; the emphasis is on recognising when tone clashes with context and negotiating a better way to say the same thing. |
| 4 | Model | Timed Opinion Turn | Learners analyse model dialogues and short monologues showing effective shifts in tone and register between audiences (friends, teachers, online posts), identifying specific linguistic choices (contractions, slang, modals, hedging, address forms) that mark formal, neutral, and informal communication. |
| 5 | Model | Mediation Brief | Students examine examples where someone explains the same issue to different audiences (e.g., a school rule to younger pupils vs to parents vs to classmates), mapping how detail, examples, and attitude signals are adapted while the key message remains stable. |
| 6 | Model | Negotiated Decision | Groups study a model discussion where teens decide how to phrase a sensitive message (apology to a teacher, request to a principal, clarification to a friend after a misunderstanding), noting how participants weigh formality, directness, and politeness to achieve the communicative goal. |
| 7 | Output | Rapid Reformulation | Learners write or record short messages for one audience (e.g., a text to a friend), then systematically rewrite or re-speak them for a different audience (e.g., teacher, school newsletter, online forum), showing controlled tone and register shifts without altering core meaning or commitments. |
| 8 | Output | Timed Opinion Turn + Mediation Brief | Students create a brief "style guide" for themselves or their class: examples of how they would talk/write to different audiences about the same topic (friend, teacher, principal, online strangers), including phrases and structures that help them sound appropriately formal, neutral, or informal. |
| 9 | Output | Negotiated Decision | Groups co-author a short set of "tone and audience" guidelines for common teen situations (disagreeing with a friend, emailing a teacher, posting about a school issue online), articulating why certain choices are appropriate for each context and how to keep messages clear but respectful. |

# Unit 5: Peer Negotiation and Problem-Solving

### Mode order: Model → Output → Performance

| Lesson | Dominant mode | Simulator patterns | Framing |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Model | Negotiated Decision | Learners analyse model peer negotiations where teens have conflicting preferences or constraints (e.g., planning group work, deciding rules for a shared space, or negotiating curfew conditions), identifying how interests, options, and trade-offs are articulated before reaching a joint decision. |
| 2 | Model | Challenge & Pushback | Students study model dialogues where one person challenges a proposal (e.g., "this plan is unfair" or "I can't agree to that schedule") and the other responds by clarifying interests and adjusting the offer; focus is on using challenge to improve solutions, not shut negotiation down. |
| 3 | Model | Mediation Brief | Learners examine examples where a third peer helps two others resolve a disagreement by summarising each side's concerns in neutral language, reframing accusatory statements, and guiding them toward shared criteria for a solution; this models C1-level mediation in negotiation contexts. |
| 4 | Output | Negotiated Decision | In groups, students design a concrete agreement for a realistic teen scenario (e.g., allocating time and tasks for a project, agreeing on social media rules in a group chat, or planning shared use of a club space), documenting each person's needs, the options considered, and the final compromise. |
| 5 | Output | Mediation Brief | Learners write or record a short neutral explanation of a peer conflict and its agreed resolution for someone who was not there (e.g., explaining to a teacher or another friend what happened and how it was solved), showing they can reconstruct the negotiation process coherently and without taking sides. |
| 6 | Output | Timed Opinion Turn | Students produce concise, structured explanations of "what we decided and why" after group problem-solving, aimed at an outside audience (such as the class or a teacher), integrating main issues, chosen solution, and key reasons, not just announcing the outcome. |
| 7 | Performance | Negotiated Decision + Challenge & Pushback | Learners engage in live peer negotiations on contested scenarios (e.g., balancing study and free time in shared plans, dealing with unfair behaviour in a group), with explicit roles for proposing, challenging, adjusting, and checking agreement; the goal is to reach specific, realistic compromises under constraints. |
| 8 | Performance | Mediation Brief | In real-time triads, one student mediates between two peers with conflicting positions, practising active listening, paraphrasing, reframing hostile language, and suggesting possible compromises for them to accept or adjust; emphasis is on keeping dialogue constructive under emotional pressure. |
| 9 | Performance | Timed Opinion Turn + Rapid Reformulation | Learners first state a firm position in a negotiation scenario, then reformulate it immediately after a counter-proposal or new information, showing they can adjust demands, propose alternatives, and maintain a collaborative tone without abandoning their core interests. |

# Unit 6: Identity, Social Issues, and Self-Expression

### Mode order: Output → Performance → Model

| Lesson | Dominant mode | Simulator patterns | Framing |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Output | Timed Opinion Turn | Learners draft and deliver short, structured pieces (spoken or recorded) on identity-adjacent topics such as "online identity vs offline self," "belonging in different groups," or "what fairness means at school," focusing on clear stance, supporting reasons, and controlled expression of feelings and attitudes. |
| 2 | Output | Mediation Brief | Students prepare explanations of a social issue or identity topic (e.g., stereotypes in media, inclusion at school, or online privacy) aimed at peers who care but are less informed, selecting and sequencing information to make complex ideas accessible without oversimplifying. |
| 3 | Output | Rapid Reformulation | Learners write or record a personal reflection on a social/identity question (for example, "one time I felt included/excluded"), then reformulate it for a different audience (friend vs teacher vs anonymous blog), practising how to adjust openness, detail, and tone while keeping the core story and message intact. |
| 4 | Performance | Timed Opinion Turn + Challenge & Pushback | In guided discussions, teens share nuanced opinions on social issues (e.g., school rules, online behaviour, identity labels) and respond in real time to respectful challenge from peers, practising hedging, acknowledging other experiences, and maintaining constructive tone when views differ. |
| 5 | Performance | Negotiated Decision | Small groups discuss and agree on simple, practical commitments related to identity and social issues in their class context (e.g., "principles for including people in group work" or "guidelines for talking about differences respectfully"), balancing different perspectives to reach shared, concrete statements. |
| 6 | Performance | Timed Opinion Turn + Mediation Brief | Learners explain how a particular identity-related situation might feel from more than one point of view (for instance, a new student joining the class, someone being stereotyped, or a friend coming out), practising empathy and social awareness while keeping the language non-intrusive and option-friendly. |
| 7 | Model | Timed Opinion Turn | Students analyse model responses where older teens express complex feelings and attitudes about identity or social topics with clarity and precision, noticing how these speakers develop ideas, use nuanced opinion phrases, and signal uncertainty or mixed feelings rather than giving simple "for/against" answers. |
| 8 | Model | Mediation Brief | Learners study examples of strong explanations of social or identity issues targeted at youth audiences (e.g., short articles, blog posts, or talks), identifying how the writer or speaker chooses details, balances information with personal voice, and avoids intrusive or over-personal storytelling. |
| 9 | Model | Rapid Reformulation | Students examine pairs of texts or clips in which the same core experience or viewpoint is expressed in different registers or levels of openness (e.g., private journal vs public post vs classroom speech), mapping what changes in phrasing, detail, and stance and how the core meaning is preserved. |
